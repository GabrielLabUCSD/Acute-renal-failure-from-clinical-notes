Patient is a 65-year-old male who has a medical history significant for hypertension and diabetes mellitus type 2. He presents to the clinic with the intention of undergoing surgery for a non-emergent condition. The patient has a history of well-controlled hypertension on a combination of lisinopril and hydrochlorothiazide. His blood pressure averages around 130/80 mmHg. He is also managed on metformin for his diabetes, with his hemoglobin A1c consistently below 7%.

The patient denies any history of heart disease, previous cardiac procedures, or myocardial infarction. He reports occasional episodes of exertional dyspnea but attributes it to his sedentary lifestyle. He has no known allergies. The patient is a lifelong non-smoker and does not consume alcohol.

His physical exam reveals a BMI of 28, regular heart sounds without murmurs, clear lung fields, and normal peripheral pulses. Laboratory investigations, including complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel, and coagulation profile, are all within normal limits. EKG shows no significant abnormalities.

Preoperatively, the patient underwent assessment by a multidisciplinary team involving an anesthesiologist, surgeon, and nursing staff. Based on his stable medical conditions, he was deemed to be at low risk for intraoperative complications. The anesthesiologist recommended general anesthesia with endotracheal intubation.

The patient underwent surgery without any intraoperative complications. The procedure was uneventful, lasting approximately two hours. Intraoperative blood loss was minimal, and no transfusion was given. A urinary catheter was placed intraoperatively, and the patient was transferred to the post-anesthesia care unit (PACU) in stable condition.

The patient had an uncomplicated postoperative course in the PACU. His pain was adequately controlled with IV opioid analgesics. He received routine postoperative care, including deep breathing exercises and early ambulation. He tolerated a clear liquid diet and had no issues with bowel function. His urinary output remained within normal limits, and he was able to void spontaneously.

The patient was closely monitored for any signs of infection or other postoperative complications. His vital signs remained stable, and he did not develop any signs of wound infection or other surgical site complications. There were no signs of thrombosis or embolic events. The patient was discharged from the hospital on postoperative day three without any complications.

In conclusion, this 65-year-old male patient with a history of hypertension and diabetes mellitus type 2 underwent non-emergent surgery without any intraoperative or postoperative complications. His postoperative course was uneventful, and he was discharged in stable condition.
