The patient is a 45-year-old male with a history of hypertension and diabetes mellitus type 2. He presents today for surgical evaluation. His hypertension is well-controlled on hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg daily. His diabetes is managed with metformin 1000 mg twice daily and his most recent HbA1c was 7.2%.

The patient has a past surgical history significant for appendectomy in childhood and tonsillectomy at age 18. He has never had any complications from previous surgeries and tolerates anesthesia well. There are no known drug allergies.

He does not smoke but occasionally drinks alcohol socially, limiting his intake to 1-2 drinks per week. He denies recreational drug use. His family history is significant for coronary artery disease in his father and mother.

The patient reports no current symptoms and denies any chest pain, shortness of breath, or palpitations. He is able to perform his activities of daily living without difficulty.

Physical examination reveals a blood pressure of 128/80 mmHg, heart rate of 72 beats per minute, and BMI of 30. His cardiovascular and respiratory exams are unremarkable. His abdomen is soft and non-tender.

Lab results show a fasting blood glucose of 130 mg/dL, creatinine of 0.9 mg/dL, and a normal complete blood count. Electrocardiogram is within normal limits.

After assessment, it is decided that the patient will undergo cholecystectomy due to chronic cholecystitis. The procedure is scheduled for next week. Preoperative instructions are given, and the patient is advised to continue his current medications up to the day of surgery.

Postoperatively, the patient does well without any complications. He is monitored in the recovery room and later transferred to the surgical floor. Vital signs remain stable, and he has no evidence of surgical site infection or wound complications. Pain is managed with oral analgesics and he is encouraged to ambulate.

The patient is discharged home on postoperative day 2. He is given a prescription for pain medication and advised to gradually resume normal activities. He is also counseled on dietary modifications to prevent gallstone formation. The patient is instructed to follow up with his primary care physician in two weeks for a postoperative check-up.
