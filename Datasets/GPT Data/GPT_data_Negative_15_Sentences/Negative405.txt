Patient XYZ is a 55-year-old male with a significant medical history. He has a history of hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidemia, and coronary artery disease. His blood pressure is well controlled with medication, and his diabetes is managed with oral hypoglycemic agents. He also takes a statin for his hyperlipidemia. His coronary artery disease is stable, and he has undergone coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) 5 years ago.

The patient has a history of smoking, although he quit 10 years ago. He has also been compliant with regular exercise and a healthy diet. He has no history of chronic kidney disease, acute renal failure, or any other significant renal pathology.

He is scheduled to undergo an elective surgical procedure for the removal of a benign mass in his abdomen. His preoperative workup is unremarkable, except for a slightly elevated fasting blood sugar level. The surgery is expected to be low risk, and the patient is expected to make a full recovery. He has been counseled about the importance of perioperative glycemic control and has been started on a short-acting insulin regimen prior to surgery.

Postoperatively, the patient's recovery has been uneventful. He is monitored closely for glycemic control, and his blood sugar levels are well managed with the help of the medical team. He is started on regular physical therapy to aid in a swift recovery. The patient's pain is managed with a multimodal approach including non-opioid analgesics. He is mobilized early and encouraged to resume his regular diet and activities as tolerated.

The patient is discharged from the hospital on the fourth postoperative day. He is advised to follow up with his primary care physician and surgical team for wound care and monitoring of his glycemic control. He is counseled on the importance of continued smoking cessation efforts and lifestyle modifications to maintain his overall cardiovascular health.

The patient does not experience any postoperative complications such as wound infection, deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, or surgical site complications. He follows up regularly with his healthcare team and adheres to medication and lifestyle recommendations. His incisions heal well without any signs of dehiscence or infection. Overall, the patient's postoperative course is favorable, and he makes a full recovery without any renal complications or acute renal failure.
