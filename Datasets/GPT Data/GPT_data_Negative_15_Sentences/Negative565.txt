The patient is a 55-year-old male with a history of hypertension and type 2 diabetes mellitus. He has a significant smoking history of 20 pack-years and quit 10 years ago. He also has a family history of coronary artery disease. He presents with symptoms of worsening angina, with exertional chest pain occurring at least twice a week.

On physical examination, he is found to have a blood pressure of 140/90 mmHg, a heart rate of 80 bpm, and normal respiratory rate. His cardiac examination reveals a regular rhythm with normal heart sounds and no murmurs. His lung examination is unremarkable.

The patient's baseline laboratory results reveal fasting blood glucose levels of 150 mg/dL and HbA1c of 7.5%. He has a total cholesterol of 200 mg/dL, LDL of 130 mg/dL, HDL of 40 mg/dL, and triglycerides of 150 mg/dL. His renal function, liver function, and electrolyte levels are all within normal limits.

A stress test and cardiac catheterization reveal three-vessel coronary artery disease, with 90% stenosis in the left anterior descending artery and 70% stenosis in the right and left circumflex arteries. Left ventricular function is found to be normal.

The decision is made to proceed with coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) surgery. The patient is thoroughly counseled about the procedure, including risks and benefits, and he provides informed consent.

The patient undergoes uncomplicated CABG surgery. The procedure consists of a saphenous vein graft to the left anterior descending artery, a radial artery graft to the right coronary artery, and a second saphenous vein graft to the left circumflex artery. The surgery is successful, and the patient is transferred to the intensive care unit for postoperative care.

During his postoperative course, the patient receives appropriate pain management, antibiotic prophylaxis, and prophylaxis against deep vein thrombosis. He is extubated on the first postoperative day and is able to ambulate with assistance. He is started on a cardiac rehabilitation program and receives appropriate counseling on lifestyle modifications, including smoking cessation and dietary changes to better manage his diabetes.

The patient does not experience any major complications during his hospital stay. He has stable vital signs, normal renal function, and no signs of infection. His chest incisions heal well, and he is discharged on postoperative day 5 with appropriate medications for his comorbidities, including aspirin, statin therapy, anti-hypertensives, and oral hypoglycemic agents. He is instructed to follow up with his cardiologist in two weeks for a comprehensive cardiac evaluation.
