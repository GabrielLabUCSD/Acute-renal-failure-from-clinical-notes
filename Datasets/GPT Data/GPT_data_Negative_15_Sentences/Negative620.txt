Patient is a 55-year-old male with a past medical history significant for hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and diabetes mellitus type II. He presents to the surgical clinic for a preoperative evaluation prior to undergoing surgery for a cholecystectomy. 

The patient's hypertension has been well controlled on lisinopril 10 mg daily and amlodipine 5 mg daily. His hyperlipidemia is managed with simvastatin 40 mg daily, and his diabetes is well controlled with metformin 1000 mg twice daily. He has no known drug allergies. 

His surgical history includes an appendectomy performed 10 years ago. He has experienced no complications from that surgery. 

His family history is notable for his father having had a myocardial infarction in his mid-60s. 

On physical examination, the patient is afebrile with blood pressure of 130/80 mmHg, heart rate of 75 beats per minute, respiratory rate of 14 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 98% on room air. His abdomen is soft and non-tender with no organomegaly or palpable masses. There are no significant findings on cardiovascular or pulmonary examination. 

Laboratory results are within normal limits, including a complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel, coagulation studies, and preoperative EKG. 

The patient is advised to continue his medications as prescribed and to refrain from eating or drinking for 8 hours prior to surgery. He is educated on the need for deep breathing and ambulation postoperatively to prevent complications such as pneumonia and deep vein thrombosis. 

The patient undergoes the cholecystectomy without any intraoperative complications. He is observed in the recovery room for an appropriate length of time and his vital signs are stable throughout. 

Postoperatively, the patient is started on a clear liquid diet and is gradually advanced to a regular diet as tolerated. He receives postoperative pain control with scheduled acetaminophen and as needed opioids. 

During his hospital stay, the patient has an uneventful recovery. He remains afebrile and his incision is clean, dry, and without signs of infection. 

On postoperative day 2, the patient is ambulating well and exhibits no shortness of breath or chest pain. His bowels have returned to normal function. 

The patient is discharged on postoperative day 3 with instructions to follow up with his primary care physician in one week and to continue regular follow-up with his surgeon in two weeks.
