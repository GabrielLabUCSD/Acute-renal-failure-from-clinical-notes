The patient is a 45-year-old female with a history of hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, and obesity. She has been on antihypertensive medications and oral hypoglycemic agents for the past five years. Her blood pressure has been well controlled, averaging around 130/80 mmHg. Her blood glucose levels are also controlled with an HbA1c of 6.5% and fasting blood sugar around 100 mg/dL.

She is scheduled for an elective surgery to remove gallstones, as she has been experiencing recurrent episodes of biliary colic over the past six months. She denies any history of jaundice or cholangitis. The patient's liver function tests are within normal limits.

The patient had previously undergone a laparoscopic cholecystectomy for gallstones five years ago and has been symptom-free until recently. She has no major comorbidities such as chronic kidney disease, heart disease, or lung disease. The patient reports no drug allergies or adverse reactions to anesthesia in the past.

Her preoperative investigations include a complete blood count, liver function test, renal function test, coagulation profile, and an ECG, all of which are within normal limits. The patient has given her informed consent for the surgery and has been counseled about the procedure, potential risks, and benefits.

The surgery is performed under general anesthesia without any intraoperative complications. The patient is transferred to the recovery room in stable condition and is closely monitored for any signs of postoperative complications. She is started on prophylactic antibiotics and thromboembolic prophylaxis.

In the postoperative period, the patient's vital signs remain stable, with no signs of infection or wound complications. She is tolerating oral intake and her pain is well controlled with analgesics. The patient is mobilized on the same day of surgery and encouraged to ambulate.

During her hospital stay, the patient is closely monitored for any signs of postoperative complications, such as wound infection, deep vein thrombosis, or respiratory compromise. However, she does not develop any of these complications.

The patient's postoperative course is uneventful, and she is discharged on the third postoperative day. She is given instructions for wound care, analgesics for pain management, and a follow-up appointment in two weeks. The patient is advised to resume her regular medications, including antihypertensive drugs and oral hypoglycemic agents.

In summary, this 45-year-old female with a history of hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, and obesity is undergoing an elective surgery for gallstones. She has no significant comorbidities and her preoperative investigations are within normal limits. The patient undergoes a successful surgery without any intraoperative complications. Her postoperative course is uneventful, and she is discharged on the third postoperative day without any complications.
