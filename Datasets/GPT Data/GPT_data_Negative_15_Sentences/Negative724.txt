His hypertension has been well-controlled, with blood pressure consistently within target range over the past year. His diabetes is adequately managed with lifestyle modifications and oral medications, maintaining a HbA1c level of 7%. His lipid profile has also improved with statin therapy, showing a reduction in LDL cholesterol levels.

The patient has a history of smoking for 30 years, but he quit smoking five years ago. He has no respiratory complaints and his lung function is normal. The patient was encouraged to ambulate early to prevent complications such as deep vein thrombosis.

He experienced mild postoperative pain, which was effectively managed with oral analgesics. The patient had a regular postoperative diet and had no issues with oral intake. He maintained good glycemic control throughout the hospital stay. There were no signs of wound infection, dehiscence, or other surgical site complications. His vital signs remained stable, and he did not require any further interventions or transfusions.

Patient X was discharged on postoperative day five in stable condition. He was advised to continue his regular medications and follow up with his primary care physician in two weeks. The patient was given clear instructions on wound care, which he showed understanding and compliance with.

In summary, Patient X, a middle-aged male with a history of hypertension, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia, underwent elective gallbladder removal surgery. He experienced an uneventful postoperative course with no complications. Thus, the surgery was successful, and the patient was discharged in stable condition.
