The patient is a 45-year-old male with a history of hypertension and type 2 diabetes mellitus. He also has a smoking history of 20 years and reports occasional alcohol consumption. He presents to the hospital with complaints of increasing pain in his right knee that is limiting his mobility and daily activities. The patient has been diagnosed with severe osteoarthritis in his right knee, and surgical intervention is recommended.

His past medical history includes a coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery three years ago due to coronary artery disease. The patient had an uneventful postoperative course after the CABG and has been on aspirin and a statin since then. He has also been receiving treatment for his hypertension with a combination of an ACE inhibitor and a diuretic, which is well-controlled.

In terms of his diabetes management, the patient is on oral hypoglycemic agents and reports good blood glucose control with a recent HbA1c level of 6.5%. He also adheres to a healthy diet and exercises regularly to maintain optimal glycemic control.

The patient's preoperative assessment reveals normal vital signs, with blood pressure well-controlled at 130/80 mmHg. Laboratory investigations show a normal complete blood count and renal function. His liver function tests and coagulation profile are also within normal limits. An electrocardiogram exhibits normal sinus rhythm.

The patient is scheduled to undergo a total knee replacement surgery. The surgical team has explained the procedure, potential risks, and benefits, and the patient provides informed consent.

Following the surgery, the patient's postoperative course is uneventful. He is closely monitored for pain control, given appropriate analgesics, and instructed on early mobilization. Physical therapy sessions are initiated to aid in the recovery process. The patient demonstrates good compliance with medication and therapy recommendations.

During the hospital stay, the patient does not experience any complications such as wound infection, deep vein thrombosis, or pulmonary embolism. His vital signs remain stable, and he does not show any signs or symptoms of surgical site infection. His blood glucose levels are closely monitored, and appropriate adjustments are made to his antidiabetic medications.

After a five-day hospitalization, the patient is discharged home with detailed postoperative instructions. He is prescribed a two-week course of prophylactic antibiotics and anticoagulants to prevent infection and thrombotic events. His pain is well-controlled with oral analgesics. The patient is advised to follow up with both his orthopedic surgeon and primary care physician in two weeks.

Overall, the patient's surgical experience was successful, and he did not encounter any complications during the postoperative period. His early mobilization and good adherence to medication and therapy recommendations might have contributed to his smooth recovery.
