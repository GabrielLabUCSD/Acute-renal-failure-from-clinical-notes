The patient is a 65-year-old male with a past medical history of hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and obesity.
He has been taking medications for blood pressure control and blood sugar management for the last 10 years.
The patient has a history of smoking for 20 years but quit 5 years ago.
He also has a family history of cardiovascular disease, with his father having a heart attack at the age of 55.
The patient has undergone a cholecystectomy 15 years ago and had no complications during or after the surgery.
He currently presents with symptomatic gallstones and is planned for elective cholecystectomy.
The patient's preoperative labs show well-controlled blood pressure and blood sugar levels.
He has no known drug allergies and has been medically optimized for surgery by his primary care physician.
During the postoperative course, the patient did well initially with no signs of infection or bleeding.
However, on the third postoperative day, he developed a fever and elevated white blood cell count.
Further workup revealed a surgical site infection, for which he was started on appropriate antibiotics.
The patient responded well to treatment, and his fever resolved within 48 hours.
He was discharged home on a course of oral antibiotics and instructed to follow up with his surgeon in 2 weeks.
At the follow-up visit, the patient's surgical site infection had completely resolved, and he was recovering well from the surgery without any further complications.
