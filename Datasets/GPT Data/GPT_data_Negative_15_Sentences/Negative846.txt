Mr. Smith, a 60-year-old male, is scheduled to undergo surgery for a hernia repair. He has a medical history significant for hypertension, well-controlled with medication. Additionally, he has a history of type 2 diabetes managed with diet and exercise. 

Mr. Smith has no known drug allergies but does have a history of smoking for 30 years, which he quit 10 years ago. He reports occasional shortness of breath with exertion, attributed to mild asthma. He leads a sedentary lifestyle and has a body mass index (BMI) of 28. 

Laboratory investigations revealed normal renal function and electrolyte levels, with a baseline serum creatinine of 0.9 mg/dL. His preoperative chest X-ray showed mild emphysematous changes. He was cleared for surgery by his primary care physician and pulmonologist.

Postoperatively, Mr. Smith experienced mild surgical site pain, which was managed effectively with oral analgesics. His vital signs remained stable, and he demonstrated good wound healing. He was encouraged to ambulate early to prevent complications and was discharged home after a successful recovery without any major postoperative issues. 

Although Mr. Smith did not experience any complications related to the surgery, he was advised to follow up with his primary care physician for routine monitoring of his chronic conditions. Overall, he had a favorable postoperative course and was satisfied with the outcome of the surgery.
