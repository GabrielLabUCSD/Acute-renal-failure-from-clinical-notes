The patient is a 58-year-old male with a medical history significant for hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and hyperlipidemia.
He is currently managed with medications including lisinopril, metformin, and atorvastatin.
The patient presents with complaints of chronic knee pain and functional impairment due to severe osteoarthritis.
After evaluation, it is determined that he would benefit from a total knee arthroplasty.
The patient has provided informed consent and has been scheduled for surgery on the right knee.
Preoperative laboratory studies are within normal limits, including renal function tests.
He does not have a history of bleeding disorders or any recent infections.
The patient is a non-smoker and denies any history of alcohol or substance abuse.
In terms of surgical risk, he has a BMI of 32, indicating mild obesity.
The patient has been advised to stop taking anticoagulant medications, such as aspirin, one week prior to surgery.
He has been instructed to continue taking his other regular medications up to the day of surgery.
The patient has received preoperative teaching regarding the surgical procedure, postoperative pain management, and rehabilitation expectations.
On the day of surgery, the patient is taken to the operating room and placed under spinal anesthesia.
The surgical procedure proceeds without complications, and a total knee arthroplasty is successfully performed.
Postoperatively, the patient is monitored closely for pain control and signs of infection.
He is started on a prophylactic antibiotic regimen to prevent surgical site infections.
Physical therapy is initiated on the first postoperative day to promote early mobilization.
The patient experiences some expected postoperative pain, which is managed with a combination of oral opioids and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs.
He gradually gains weight-bearing tolerance and is able to ambulate with the assistance of a walker.
The patient is discharged home on the fourth postoperative day with instructions for at-home exercises and pain management.
He is advised to follow up with the surgeon after two weeks for wound check and suture removal.
During the postoperative course, the patient does not develop any signs of infection or wound dehiscence.
He reports gradual improvement in pain and functional abilities, and his incision site is healing well.
At the two-week follow-up visit, the surgeon is pleased with the patient's progress and recommends continuing with physical therapy.
Overall, the patient had a successful surgery and is recovering well without any significant complications.
