They have been managing these conditions with medication, including angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, oral hypoglycemic agents, and statins. Their renal function tests are also within normal range, with a serum creatinine level of 0.9 mg/dL. The patient is consented for surgery and evaluated by an anesthesiologist. Preoperative laboratory tests including coagulation profile are within normal limits, and the patient is cleared for surgery. The anesthesiologist decides to use a general anesthetic along with endotracheal intubation.

The patient undergoes surgery and has a relatively uneventful procedure. Intraoperatively, no complications are noted, and the estimated blood loss is minimal. The surgery lasts for several hours, during which the patient is monitored closely for any adverse events or hemodynamic instability.

Following the surgery, the patient is taken to the post-anesthesia care unit (PACU) for recovery. They are monitored for vital signs, pain management, and signs of any complications. The patient's pain is managed adequately with intravenous analgesics and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs. They are encouraged to ambulate and perform respiratory exercises to prevent postoperative complications such as deep vein thrombosis and atelectasis.

Over the next few days, the patient's condition gradually improves. Their vital signs remain stable, their pain decreases, and they are able to tolerate oral intake. Monitoring of blood glucose levels reveals adequate glycemic control, and their medications are adjusted slightly to maintain optimal glycemic levels. The patient's surgical wound is assessed regularly, showing signs of healthy healing without any signs of infection.

Throughout their hospital stay, the patient is provided with deep vein thrombosis prophylaxis in the form of intermittent pneumatic compression devices and early mobilization. They are also closely monitored for any signs of respiratory distress, infection, or other postoperative complications. The patient is supported with intravenous fluids to maintain hydration and electrolyte balance.

By the fifth day, the patient's pain is well controlled with oral analgesics, and they are able to tolerate a regular diet. They are discharged from the hospital with appropriate postoperative instructions and a follow-up appointment scheduled for wound assessment. The patient is advised to continue their regular medications, maintain a healthy lifestyle, and monitor their blood glucose levels closely.

Postoperatively, no significant complications are noted in the patient's medical record. They do not develop any surgical site infections, respiratory complications, urinary retention, or cardiovascular events. Their postoperative course is relatively uneventful, and they recover well, as expected for a patient without any major comorbidities.
