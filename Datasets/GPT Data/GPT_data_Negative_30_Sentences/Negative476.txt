The patient is a 45-year-old male who has a past medical history significant for hypertension and type 2 diabetes mellitus.
He is scheduled to undergo surgery for appendicitis, which is an emergency procedure due to the patient's worsening condition.
The patient has been experiencing right lower abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting for the past two days.
Physical examination revealed tenderness in the right lower quadrant of the abdomen with rebound tenderness and guarding.
Blood work showed an elevated white blood cell count and C-reactive protein levels, indicating inflammation.
A computed tomography scan confirmed the diagnosis of acute appendicitis with no evidence of perforation.
The surgical team recommended an open appendectomy due to the patient's presentation and the absence of laparoscopic expertise.
The patient was admitted to the hospital on an emergency basis and started on intravenous antibiotics and hydration.
Preoperative counseling was provided to the patient and informed consent for the procedure was obtained.
The patient underwent an uncomplicated open appendectomy under general anesthesia.
During the procedure, the inflamed appendix was identified, ligated, and removed.
The surgical site was irrigated and closed with sutures.
Post-operatively, the patient was closely monitored for any signs of infection, wound complications, or postoperative ileus.
Pain control was achieved with opioids initially and transitioned to oral analgesics as tolerated.
The patient was started on a clear liquid diet on the first postoperative day and advanced to regular diet as tolerated.
He was instructed to restrict physical activities and avoid heavy lifting for the next two weeks.
The patient's vital signs remained stable throughout the postoperative course.
There were no signs of surgical site infection, such as redness, warmth, swelling, or purulent drainage.
The patient's bowel movements resumed on the second postoperative day, indicating normal bowel function.
He was able to ambulate independently and was in minimal pain.
The surgical team conducted regular wound care and observed the incision site for proper healing.
The patient's blood glucose levels were closely monitored, and his diabetes medications were adjusted as per the endocrinologist's recommendations.
The patient's overall postoperative recovery was uneventful, and he was discharged on the third postoperative day.
He was provided with discharge instructions, including wound care, medication management, and follow-up appointments.
During the follow-up visit at two weeks post-surgery, the patient reported complete resolution of abdominal pain and was healing well.
No complications, such as wound dehiscence, abscess formation, or incisional hernias, were observed.
The patient was advised to continue monitoring his blood pressure and blood glucose levels, as well as to maintain a healthy lifestyle.
Overall, the patient had an uncomplicated surgical course and a successful recovery.
