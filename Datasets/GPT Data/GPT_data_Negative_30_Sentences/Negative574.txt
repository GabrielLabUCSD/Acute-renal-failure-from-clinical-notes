The patient is a 55-year-old male with a history of hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and obesity. He presents to the hospital for evaluation and preparation for surgery. His surgical procedure will be a laparoscopic cholecystectomy due to symptomatic cholelithiasis. The patient has been experiencing recurrent episodes of right upper quadrant pain and has failed conservative management. He has a history of multiple gallbladder attacks in the past year.

His medical history is significant for hypertension, which is well-controlled with medication. He takes a daily dose of lisinopril, amlodipine, and hydrochlorothiazide. His blood pressure has been within the normal range during recent office visits. The patient also has type 2 diabetes, which is managed with metformin and diet modification. His hemoglobin A1c levels have been stable at around 6.5%.

In addition to hypertension and diabetes, the patient is obese with a body mass index (BMI) of 35. He has made prior attempts to lose weight but has had minimal success. He has been referred to a nutritionist to help with weight management.

On physical examination, the patient's vital signs are stable. He is in no acute distress but is found to have a distended abdomen with tenderness in the right upper quadrant upon palpation. There is no guarding or rebound tenderness. Laboratory workup including complete blood count and liver function tests are within normal limits.

The patient is currently on aspirin 81 mg daily for primary prevention of cardiovascular disease. This has been continued preoperatively to decrease the risk of postoperative thromboembolism.

He has provided informed consent for the procedure and has been instructed on preoperative preparations, including fasting prior to surgery. The patient has been counseled about the risks and benefits of surgery and has expressed understanding.

The patient underwent laparoscopic cholecystectomy without any complications. The procedure was performed under general anesthesia with no intraoperative complications noted. The gallbladder was found to contain multiple stones, and the surgery was successful in removing it completely. The patient tolerated the procedure well and was transferred to the recovery room.

In the immediate postoperative period, the patient was monitored closely for any signs of bleeding, infection, or other complications. He was started on a clear liquid diet and advanced to regular diet as tolerated. Pain was managed with intravenous opioids initially and later switched to oral analgesics.

The patient's postoperative course was uneventful, and he was discharged home on the third postoperative day. He was instructed to continue taking his regular medications, including the lisinopril, amlodipine, hydrochlorothiazide, metformin, and aspirin. He was advised to follow up with his primary care physician within a week for wound check and further evaluation of his hypertension and type 2 diabetes.

There were no immediate complications such as wound infection, bleeding, or respiratory distress. He did not require any additional interventions during his hospital stay. The patient was given postoperative care instructions, including signs and symptoms to watch for and when to seek immediate medical attention.

In the outpatient follow-up visit, the patient's surgical incisions were healing well with no signs of infection or dehiscence. His blood pressure was within the normal range, and he reported good glycemic control. He was encouraged to continue lifestyle modifications to promote weight loss.

Overall, the patient had a successful laparoscopic cholecystectomy without any significant postoperative complications. He was satisfied with the outcome and expressed relief from his preoperative symptoms. The patient was advised to maintain regular follow-ups with his healthcare providers to monitor his chronic conditions and ensure optimal long-term health.
