Patient X is a 55-year-old male who is scheduled for surgery. He has a medical history significant for hypertension, dyslipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. His blood pressure has been well controlled on lisinopril 10 mg daily. His lipid panel shows elevated LDL cholesterol, which is managed with atorvastatin 20 mg daily. His diabetes is also well controlled with metformin 1000 mg twice daily.

He has a past surgical history of appendectomy and cholecystectomy, both of which were uncomplicated. He does not have any known drug allergies.

Patient X's preoperative laboratory results show normal liver and renal function, with a serum creatinine level of 0.9 mg/dL. His electrocardiogram (ECG) shows normal sinus rhythm, with no evidence of ischemia or arrhythmias. He has a negative stress test within the past year.

His chest X-ray shows no acute abnormalities, and his pulmonary function tests indicate normal lung function. He is a non-smoker and denies any history of chronic lung disease.

Patient X is scheduled to have elective surgery for a hernia repair. He has been advised to discontinue his atorvastatin 5 days before surgery, as per the surgeon's instructions. He is also instructed to continue his metformin and lisinopril as usual.

In the postoperative period, Patient X is initially managed with intravenous (IV) fluids and pain control. He is started on prophylactic antibiotics to prevent infection. He is encouraged to ambulate as tolerated to prevent postoperative complications such as deep vein thrombosis.

On the first postoperative day, Patient X has stable vital signs and reports mild pain at the surgical site. His IV fluids are discontinued, and he is started on an oral diet as tolerated. His surgical wound is assessed and found to be clean and dry, with no signs of infection.

Over the next few days, Patient X's pain gradually improves, and he is able to tolerate regular meals without any gastrointestinal issues. He is instructed to continue taking his regular medications, including metformin and lisinopril.

At the time of discharge, Patient X is provided with appropriate wound care instructions and advised to follow up with his primary care physician in one week for suture removal. He is also given a prescription for pain medication as needed.

Patient X's postoperative course is uneventful, with no complications noted during his hospital stay. He is recovering well and is satisfied with the outcome of the surgery. There were no signs of infection or wound dehiscence during the follow-up visit with his primary care physician.

In conclusion, Patient X, a 55-year-old male with a history of hypertension, dyslipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus, underwent elective surgery for a hernia repair. His postoperative course was uneventful, and he had an uncomplicated recovery. No complications, including acute renal failure, were observed during his hospital stay or follow-up visit.
