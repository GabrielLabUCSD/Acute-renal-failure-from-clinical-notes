The patient, a 65-year-old male, presented to the hospital for surgery. He has a past medical history significant for hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, and coronary artery disease. The patient had previously undergone a coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery 5 years ago. He is a current smoker, with a 30-pack-year smoking history, and drinks alcohol occasionally.

Upon further investigation, the patient's preoperative laboratory results indicate well-controlled blood glucose levels, with a hemoglobin A1c of 6.5%. His renal function is within normal limits, with a creatinine level of 0.9 mg/dL. The patient's blood pressure is adequately managed with a combination of angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor and a diuretic, and his last recorded blood pressure was 130/80 mmHg.

In terms of surgical indications, the patient requires surgery for a hernia repair. He has been symptomatic for several months, experiencing pain and discomfort at the site of the hernia. Upon physical examination, there is a visible bulge, and the patient reports pain on palpation.

During the preoperative evaluation, it was noted that the patient has a history of an allergic reaction to penicillin, with the development of a rash, and therefore, it was documented as an allergy in his medical record. This information was communicated to the surgical team and appropriate precautions were planned.

The patient underwent surgery successfully with no intraoperative complications. The hernia repair procedure was uneventful, and the patient was extubated at the end of surgery. He was gradually weaned off intravenous pain medications and transitioned to an oral analgesic regimen to manage postoperative pain.

During the postoperative period, the patient received routine surgical wound care, which included regular dressing changes and monitoring for signs of infection. The patient's pain was adequately managed with oral analgesics. He was encouraged to ambulate early to prevent postoperative complications such as deep vein thrombosis. The patient tolerated oral intake well and there were no concerns of postoperative nausea or vomiting.

In terms of complications, the patient did not experience any wound complications such as dehiscence or hematoma formation. Additionally, there were no signs of surgical site infection or systemic infection. Postoperatively, the patient's blood pressure remained stable, and there were no new cardiac concerns identified.

The patient's postoperative course was uncomplicated, and he was discharged home on postoperative day 3. He was advised to continue his regular medications, including antihypertensive and antidiabetic medications. Additionally, the patient was advised to abstain from smoking to optimize healing and reduce the risk of postoperative complications.

Follow-up arrangements were made with the patient's primary care physician and surgeon. An appointment was scheduled for two weeks post-surgery to evaluate the surgical wound and monitor for any delayed complications. The patient was educated on signs and symptoms to report and instructed to seek medical attention if any concerns arise before the scheduled follow-up appointment.

In conclusion, this 65-year-old male patient successfully underwent hernia repair surgery without any immediate complications. His postoperative course was unremarkable, and he was discharged home on postoperative day 3. The patient was diligent in following postoperative instructions and was advised to continue primary prevention measures for his hypertension and diabetes. With appropriate follow-up and adherence to medical recommendations, he is expected to have a full recovery.
