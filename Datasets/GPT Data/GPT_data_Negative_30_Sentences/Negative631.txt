The patient is a 45-year-old male who is planning to undergo surgery for a hernia repair. He has a past medical history significant for hypertension and type 2 diabetes, both of which are well controlled with medications. The patient is a non-smoker and denies any current or prior alcohol or substance abuse.

On examination, the patient is found to be generally in good health with stable vital signs. Abdominal examination reveals a reducible umbilical hernia without any signs of bowel obstruction or strangulation. Laboratory investigations reveal a normal complete blood count and renal function. His HbA1c level is within target range, indicating good glycemic control.

The patient's surgical history includes an appendectomy performed 10 years ago without any complications. He had received adequate anesthesia for the procedure and had an uneventful recovery. No further surgeries have been performed since then.

During the preoperative evaluation, the patient is found to have an allergy to penicillin, which is noted and appropriately documented in his medical record.

Following surgery, the patient's postoperative course is unremarkable. He is closely monitored for any signs of infection or wound complications, but none are noted. The patient is started on a clear liquid diet and is able to tolerate it well without any gastrointestinal discomfort or complications. He is encouraged to mobilize early and performs activities of daily living without any assistance.

The patient is discharged from the hospital on postoperative day 2 after meeting all the discharge criteria. He is provided with written and verbal instructions regarding wound care, pain management, and follow-up appointments. The patient understands the instructions and expresses willingness to comply.

During the postoperative period, the patient experiences some mild pain at the incision site, which is adequately managed with oral analgesics. He follows up with his primary care physician as scheduled, and the wound is found to be healing well without any signs of infection or dehiscence. The patient is advised to restrict heavy lifting and strenuous activities for a few weeks until complete recovery.

Fortunately, the patient does not experience any complications during his postoperative course. He does not develop any postoperative infections, bowel obstructions, or other surgical site-related issues. His recovery is uneventful, and he continues to have good glucose control with his antidiabetic medications.

In summary, the 45-year-old male patient with a history of hypertension and type 2 diabetes is planning to undergo surgery for a hernia repair. He has a well-controlled medical history and denies any substance abuse or current smoking. The patient had an unremarkable surgical history and had an appendectomy performed without complications. Preoperatively, he is found to have a penicillin allergy. Postoperatively, his course is unremarkable, and he is discharged on postoperative day 2. The patient experiences mild pain and follows up with his primary care physician, demonstrating good wound healing with no complications observed.
