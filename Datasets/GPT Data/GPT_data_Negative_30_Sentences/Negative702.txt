The patient is a 55-year-old female with a past medical history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and diabetes mellitus type 2.
She is currently on medications including lisinopril, atorvastatin, and metformin.
She presents to our clinic with complaints of worsening knee pain and limited mobility due to severe osteoarthritis.
After conservative management failure, she has decided to undergo total knee replacement surgery.
Preoperative investigations include a complete blood count, renal function tests, liver function tests, and an electrocardiogram.
Her hemoglobin, creatinine, and liver enzymes are within normal limits, while her random blood glucose levels are mildly elevated.
She has been advised to continue her home medications on the day of surgery, except for metformin, which should be held on the morning of the procedure.
The patient will receive spinal anesthesia for the surgery and has been consented regarding the risks and benefits.
She underwent the surgery without any complications and was discharged from the hospital on the fifth postoperative day.
Her postoperative course was unremarkable, with controlled pain and gradual improvement in mobility.
She was provided with physical therapy exercises to aid in her rehabilitation.
The patient's wound showed no signs of infection or delayed healing.
She was able to ambulate with the help of a walker and was advised on proper weight-bearing precautions.
She was also given instructions on how to prevent the formation of blood clots through exercise and regular ankle movements.
The patient was scheduled for a follow-up appointment two weeks after discharge to assess her progress.
At the follow-up visit, she reported a significant reduction in knee pain and increased range of motion.
Her wound was well-healed with no signs of infection.
The patient's physical therapy progress was satisfactory, and she was able to walk short distances without assistance.
She did not experience any complications such as deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism.
Her blood glucose levels were monitored, and she was advised on appropriate diet and medication adjustments to maintain glycemic control.
The patient was able to taper off her pain medications and resume her regular diabetic and antihypertensive medications.
At the three-month follow-up, the patient reported a further improvement in knee function and continued to progress well with physical therapy.
Her wound had completely healed, and no signs of infection were observed.
She had resumed her daily activities and regained her independence in mobility.
The patient's blood pressure and blood glucose levels were consistently within target ranges.
Overall, the patient had a successful surgery and an uneventful postoperative course with no major complications observed.
