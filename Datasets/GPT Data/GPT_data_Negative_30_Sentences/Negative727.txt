The patient is a 45-year-old male who is scheduled to have surgery for an inguinal hernia repair. He has a past medical history significant for hypertension, dyslipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. The patient is a nonsmoker and denies any history of alcohol or illicit drug use.

The patient's blood pressure has been well controlled on a combination of lisinopril and hydrochlorothiazide. His dyslipidemia is managed with atorvastatin, and his diabetes is controlled with metformin. His last HbA1c level was 6.7%.

The patient does not have any known drug allergies. He is up to date on all of his vaccinations, including tetanus and pneumococcal vaccines. He received a flu shot last year.

His surgical history includes an appendectomy performed in childhood and a tonsillectomy as an adult. He had no complications from either surgery.

The patient's preoperative laboratory results were within normal limits. His complete blood count showed normal hemoglobin, hematocrit, and white blood cell count. His coagulation profile showed normal prothrombin time and activated partial thromboplastin time.

He has an unremarkable family history as well, with no known genetic disorders or familial predisposition to anesthesia-related complications.

The patient's physical examination revealed an otherwise healthy individual with a body mass index of 27. His cardiovascular examination demonstrated regular rate and rhythm, without any murmurs, gallops, or rubs. His lungs were clear to auscultation bilaterally.

An electrocardiogram showed normal sinus rhythm with no evidence of ischemic changes or arrhythmias. His chest X-ray was unremarkable, with clear lung fields and no evidence of cardiomegaly.

The patient was cleared for surgery by both his primary care physician and an anesthesiologist. He will have general anesthesia for his procedure.

Following the surgery, the patient had an uneventful postoperative course and was able to tolerate a regular diet. He was discharged home on the same day as his surgery. He was given prescriptions for postoperative pain management and instructed to follow up with his surgeon in two weeks.

At his follow-up appointment, the patient reported minimal pain at the incision site, which was well healing. His vital signs were stable, and no signs of infection were noted. The surgeon deemed him to be healing well and advised him to gradually resume his normal activities.

The patient did not experience any postoperative complications, such as wound infection, hematoma, or seroma formation. He did not require any additional interventions or hospital readmission.

In summary, this 45-year-old male with a history of hypertension, dyslipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus underwent a successful inguinal hernia repair. His preoperative evaluation was unremarkable, and he had an uneventful postoperative course without any complications. He has been recovering well and experienced minimal pain at the surgical site. He will continue to follow up with his surgeon for further monitoring and guidance.
