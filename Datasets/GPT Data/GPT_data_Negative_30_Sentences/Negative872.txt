The patient is a 55-year-old male who is planning to have surgery. He has a significant past medical history including hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidemia, and osteoarthritis of the knees. His blood pressure has been well controlled with medication for the past five years. His diabetes is managed with oral medication and diet modification, and he has consistent good control with a hemoglobin A1c of 6.8%. His hyperlipidemia is also well managed with a statin and his lipid profile is within normal limits.

The patient has a history of smoking in the past but quit 10 years ago. He denies any respiratory symptoms and has no history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease or asthma. He has a body mass index (BMI) of 28, which falls into the overweight category. He reports occasional knee pain and stiffness due to osteoarthritis, but he is able to manage it with over-the-counter analgesics and activity modification.

The patient has a previous surgical history of a cholecystectomy 15 years ago. He had an uneventful postoperative course with no complications. He has not undergone any major surgeries since then. He has no known drug allergies and is taking a daily multivitamin and omega-3 fatty acid supplement.

In terms of family history, his mother had a history of hypertension and his father had a myocardial infarction at the age of 65. The patient's siblings are healthy and he has no known family history of malignancies or genetic disorders.

His preoperative labs including complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel, coagulation studies, and urinalysis are all within normal limits. His chest X-ray shows no significant abnormalities. An electrocardiogram is ordered and reveals normal sinus rhythm and no evidence of ischemia.

The patient is consented for surgery and agrees to the procedure after discussing the risks and benefits with his surgeon. He is scheduled for surgery in two weeks and is advised to continue taking his regular medications up until the day of surgery.

Postoperatively, the patient recovers well in the surgical ward. He is monitored closely for any signs of infection or complications. He is started on prophylactic antibiotics as per the surgical protocol and receives adequate pain control through a combination of oral analgesics and patient-controlled analgesia.

The patient's incision site is inspected daily for signs of surgical site infection. Wound care is provided as needed, and there are no signs of dehiscence or necrosis. He is encouraged to engage in early ambulation to prevent postoperative complications such as deep vein thrombosis.

His diet is gradually advanced from clear liquids to solid foods as tolerated. There are no issues with postoperative nausea or vomiting. The patient's blood glucose levels are monitored closely, and insulin is administered as needed to maintain euglycemia.

Physical therapy is initiated to help the patient regain strength and mobility. He makes good progress and is able to walk with minimal assistance by the third postoperative day. He is educated on proper pain management techniques, wound care, and signs of potential complications.

The patient's postoperative course is uneventful, with no major complications. He is discharged home on postoperative day five after demonstrating adequate pain control, independence in activities of daily living, and a stable physiological condition. A follow-up appointment is scheduled for two weeks to monitor his progress and assess wound healing.

In summary, the patient, a 55-year-old male, is scheduled for surgery. He has a medical history significant for hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidemia, and osteoarthritis of the knees. He has well-controlled blood pressure, diabetes, and lipid levels. He is a non-smoker with a previous cholecystectomy. His preoperative labs and imaging studies are within normal limits. Postoperatively, he has an uneventful recovery with no complications. He is discharged home in stable condition, and a follow-up appointment is scheduled for two weeks.
