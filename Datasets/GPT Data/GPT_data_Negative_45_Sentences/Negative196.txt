The patient is a 55-year-old male who presented to the hospital with the plan to undergo surgery. He has a medical history significant for hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, and hyperlipidemia. His blood pressure has been well controlled on a combination of lisinopril and amlodipine. He has been managing his diabetes with diet and exercise, without the need for medication. His lipid profile has also been within the target range since starting a statin medication two years ago.

Additionally, the patient has a history of asthma, for which he uses an inhaler on an as-needed basis. He has seasonal allergies and takes loratadine daily during allergy seasons. He reports occasional episodes of acid reflux but has been managing it with over-the-counter antacids.

The patient has a social history of smoking in the past but has quit ten years ago. He denies any current alcohol or illicit drug use. His family history is significant for coronary artery disease, with both parents undergoing coronary artery bypass grafting in their 60s. He is married and lives with his wife, who is supportive of his healthcare needs.

During preoperative evaluation, the patient's physical examination was unremarkable. His laboratory tests, including complete blood count, electrolyte panel, and liver function tests, were within normal limits. His glycosylated hemoglobin (HbA1c) was also well controlled at 6.5%.

The patient was seen and evaluated by the anesthesia team, who cleared him for surgery. The surgical procedure planned is an elective cholecystectomy for symptomatic cholelithiasis, as confirmed by imaging studies. The patient has been experiencing intermittent right upper quadrant abdominal pain and occasional episodes of nausea and vomiting.

Postoperatively, the patient had an uneventful recovery period in the hospital. His vital signs remained stable, and his pain was well controlled with analgesics. The patient was able to tolerate a regular diet without any gastrointestinal discomfort. He ambulated without assistance and showed no signs of infection at the incision site.

The patient was discharged home on postoperative day three and given instructions regarding wound care, medication management, and activity restrictions. He was prescribed acetaminophen for pain relief as needed and was advised to follow-up with his surgeon for a wound check in one week.

Follow-up examination in the surgeon's office showed that the surgical incision was healing well with no signs of infection. The patient reported minimal pain and was able to resume his daily activities without limitations. His diet was gradually advanced, and he had no gastrointestinal complaints.

Throughout the postoperative course, the patient did not experience any complications, such as wound dehiscence, bleeding, infection, or respiratory distress. He had no signs of deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism. His blood glucose levels remained stable, and there were no signs of diabetic complications.

In summary, this 55-year-old male with a history of hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, hyperlipidemia, asthma, and acid reflux underwent an uneventful cholecystectomy for symptomatic cholelithiasis. He had an uncomplicated postoperative course, with adequate pain control and no surgical site infection. The patient's wound healed well, and he resumed his usual activities without limitations.
