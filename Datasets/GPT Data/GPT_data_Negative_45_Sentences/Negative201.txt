He presents for surgery with complaints of chronic pain in his right knee, which has been worsening over the past year. The pain limits his ability to perform daily activities and has failed to respond to conservative measures such as physical therapy, medications, and joint injections. 

On further evaluation, the patient has been diagnosed with severe osteoarthritis of the right knee. X-rays show significant joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, and osteophyte formation. The patient has also developed effusion within the joint, with accompanying synovitis. 

In addition to his knee pain, the patient reports occasional shortness of breath with exertion, which has led to a diagnosis of mild congestive heart failure. His ejection fraction is reduced to 45%, and he has been placed on medication to manage his symptoms. 

The patient's cardiac workup included an echocardiogram, which revealed mild aortic stenosis with a valve area of 1.2 cm^2. His pulmonary function tests were within normal limits, ruling out any significant respiratory conditions. He is a non-smoker and denies any history of asthma or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. 

The patient has also undergone routine blood tests, which showed a normal complete blood count, liver and kidney function tests, and coagulation profile. His fasting blood glucose levels were in the prediabetic range, but he is carefully monitoring his diet and exercising regularly to manage his blood sugar levels. 

During the preoperative assessment, the patient mentioned his allergy to penicillin, which has been well-documented in his medical records. He has experienced anaphylaxis in the past and carries an epinephrine auto-injector for emergencies. The surgical team has taken note of this and will avoid using any medications containing penicillin during the procedure. 

The patient's medication list includes aspirin for secondary prevention of cardiovascular events, a statin for lipid management, and lisinopril for blood pressure control. He has been instructed to discontinue the aspirin one week before the surgery to minimize bleeding risk. 

The patient's body mass index (BMI) is within the normal range, indicating that he is not obese. He maintains an active lifestyle and regularly engages in low-impact exercises, such as swimming and cycling, to manage his weight and maintain cardiovascular fitness. 

The planned surgical procedure for the patient is a total knee replacement, which will involve removing the damaged joint surfaces and replacing them with prosthetic components. The surgery will be performed under general anesthesia, and the patient is aware of the potential risks and benefits. 

Postoperative care for this patient will include pain management with a combination of opioid and non-opioid analgesics. Physical therapy will begin on the same day of surgery, aiming to improve joint mobility and muscle strength. 

To prevent deep vein thrombosis, the patient will receive prophylactic anticoagulation in the form of subcutaneous heparin injections. Sequential compression devices will also be used on his legs during surgery and throughout his hospital stay.

Physical therapy will continue after discharge, with a focus on regaining full range of motion, strengthening the surrounding muscles, and improving gait mechanics. 

The patient will be advised to avoid high-impact activities and heavy lifting for six weeks following the surgery. 

Given the patient's mild congestive heart failure, extra caution will be taken to monitor his fluid balance and optimize his diuretic therapy. 

Nutritionally, the patient's diet will be optimized to ensure adequate protein intake for wound healing and tissue repair. 

The patient's fluid intake will be carefully managed to prevent both dehydration and fluid overload. Daily weights will be obtained to monitor for any significant fluid shifts. 

The patient's cardiac medications, including beta-blockers and diuretics, will be continued postoperatively to manage his congestive heart failure. 

Regular follow-up appointments will be scheduled to monitor the patient's progress, wound healing, pain control, and any potential complications. 

Complications that can arise from total knee replacement surgery include infection, deep vein thrombosis, blood clots, nerve or blood vessel injury, and stiffness of the joint.
