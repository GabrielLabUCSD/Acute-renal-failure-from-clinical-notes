The patient is a 55-year-old male who is planning to undergo surgery. He has a past medical history of diabetes mellitus type 2, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and peripheral artery disease. His diabetes is well-controlled with oral medications, and his blood pressure and cholesterol levels are within normal limits.

He also has a history of smoking for 30 years, but quit five years ago. The patient denies any recent tobacco or alcohol use. He has a family history of heart disease, with his father suffering a myocardial infarction at the age of 65.

The patient had a recent echocardiogram which showed normal left ventricular function and no significant valvular abnormalities. His current medications include metformin, lisinopril, atorvastatin, and aspirin.

On physical examination, his vital signs are stable. His BMI is in the overweight range at 28. He has a soft systolic murmur but denies any exertional chest pain or shortness of breath. His peripheral pulses are palpable and symmetrical bilaterally.

His laboratory results reveal a fasting blood glucose level of 120 mg/dL, HbA1c of 6.8%, total cholesterol 190 mg/dL, low-density lipoprotein (LDL) 115 mg/dL, high-density lipoprotein (HDL) 45 mg/dL, and triglycerides 150 mg/dL. His renal function tests are within normal limits.

The patient is scheduled for surgery under general anesthesia. Preoperative assessment by the anesthesiologist has identified no contraindications to surgery. The surgical procedure will involve an open cholecystectomy to remove his symptomatic gallbladder stones.

Postoperatively, the patient recovers well from anesthesia without any complications. He has adequate pain control and his vital signs remain stable. The surgical wound is clean and there are no signs of infection or dehiscence.

The patient is started on a clear liquid diet and tolerated it without any issues. His blood glucose levels are monitored closely and maintained within target range. The surgical team ensures early ambulation and respiratory hygiene to prevent postoperative complications such as deep vein thrombosis and pneumonia.

On the second postoperative day, the patient complains of mild incisional pain and is started on oral analgesics. His urine output is within normal limits, and he does not exhibit any signs of acute renal failure or electrolyte imbalances.

The patient's postoperative course remains uneventful, and he is discharged on the fourth postoperative day in stable condition. He is advised to continue his home medications, maintain a healthy lifestyle, and follow up with his primary care physician for routine monitoring.

No immediate or delayed complications are noted during the hospital stay. The patient is educated about signs and symptoms of possible complications such as wound infection, fever, or worsening pain and is advised to seek medical attention if any concerns arise.

In conclusion, this patient with a medical history of diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and peripheral artery disease is planning to undergo an open cholecystectomy. His preoperative evaluation reveals stable cardiovascular status and well-controlled chronic diseases. The surgery is performed without any complications, and the patient has a smooth postoperative course. He is discharged home in stable condition, advised on self-care, and instructed to follow up with his primary care physician for continued management.
