Patient is a 55-year-old male with a medical history significant for hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus.

He presents to the clinic for preoperative evaluation as he is planning to undergo surgery for a hernia repair.

The patient reports being on antihypertensive medication for the past five years and having good blood pressure control.

He has had no recent hospitalizations and denies any history of heart disease, stroke, or arrhythmias.

His current medications include metoprolol, lisinopril, atorvastatin, and metformin.

The patient mentions that he has been following a diabetic diet and monitoring his blood sugars regularly.

He denies any symptoms of chest pain, shortness of breath, or lower extremity edema.

His physical examination reveals a well-controlled blood pressure of 130/80 mmHg and a regular heart rate.

There are no murmurs, rubs, or gallops appreciated on cardiac auscultation.

The patient's abdomen is soft, non-tender, and without any masses or hernias noted.

Laboratory results reveal a fasting blood sugar of 110 mg/dL, total cholesterol of 180 mg/dL, and a creatinine level of 1.0 mg/dL.

Renal function is within normal limits, and there are no signs of acute kidney injury.

His renal ultrasound, performed six months ago for routine screening, showed no abnormalities.

Electrocardiogram is within normal limits, showing normal sinus rhythm.

The patient's preoperative chest X-ray does not reveal any significant abnormalities.

Pulmonary function tests were not ordered as they are not routinely indicated for hernia repair.

The patient is scheduled to undergo an open hernia repair with mesh placement under general anesthesia.

He is advised to continue taking his antihypertensive, lipid-lowering, and antidiabetic medications until the morning of surgery.

The patient is instructed to have nothing by mouth after midnight on the night before the surgery.

He is educated about the procedure, potential risks, and expected postoperative course.

Intraoperatively, the surgery proceeds without complications, and the hernia is repaired successfully.

The patient's vital signs remain stable throughout the procedure, and there are no significant changes noted on intraoperative monitoring.

He is awakened from anesthesia without incident and is transferred to the post-anesthesia care unit.

In the postoperative period, the patient's pain is well-controlled with analgesics.

He is encouraged to ambulate and participate in breathing exercises to prevent complications such as deep vein thrombosis and atelectasis.

The patient's vital signs are monitored regularly, and there are no signs of postoperative infection.

He is started on a clear liquid diet and is able to tolerate it without any difficulties.

The patient's blood sugars remain stable, and his antidiabetic medications are resumed as per his routine schedule.

He is discharged home on postoperative day two with clear instructions on wound care and activity restrictions.

The patient is advised to follow up with his primary care physician in two weeks for a wound check.

During the postoperative course, the patient does not develop any significant complications.

He does not experience any wound dehiscence, infection, or mesh-related issues.

The patient's vital signs, blood pressure, and blood sugars continue to remain within normal limits.

He is compliant with his postoperative care instructions and attends the scheduled follow-up appointment.

At the follow-up visit, the wound is healing well with no signs of infection or delayed wound healing.

The patient reports minimal pain and is able to gradually increase his physical activity.

He is counseled on scar management and advised to continue his regular diabetic and hypertension management.

The patient is instructed to call the clinic if he experiences any signs of wound infection or worsening symptoms.

Overall, the patient had an uneventful postoperative course with successful hernia repair and no complications.
