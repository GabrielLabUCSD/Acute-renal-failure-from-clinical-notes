Patient is a 45-year-old male who is planning to have surgery. He has a medical history significant for hypertension, controlled on medication, and type 2 diabetes, well managed with diet and exercise. His surgical indication is a hernia repair. 

In terms of his preoperative evaluation, the patient had a complete blood count (CBC) and comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP), which were within normal limits. He also had an electrocardiogram (ECG) which showed no significant abnormalities. His chest X-ray revealed no signs of active cardiopulmonary disease. 

The patient's past surgical history is significant for a laparoscopic cholecystectomy performed ten years ago. He did not have any complications or adverse events related to this procedure. Furthermore, he has no known drug allergies. 

During the preoperative assessment, the patient reported no ongoing symptoms or complaints. He denied any significant weight loss or gain. His exercise tolerance was good, and he was able to perform his activities of daily living without difficulty. 

The patient does not smoke cigarettes, but he does admit to consuming alcohol occasionally, with an average of one to two drinks per week. He has never used illicit drugs. 

His family history is significant for hypertension in both parents and a brother who had a successful hernia repair procedure. There is no family history of surgical complications or adverse reactions to anesthesia. 

All routine preoperative vaccinations were up to date, including tetanus, influenza, and pneumococcal vaccines. The patient also received oral prophylactic antibiotics one hour prior to surgery. 

On the day of surgery, the patient was brought to the operating room without any complications. Anesthesia induction was uneventful, and the patient was intubated successfully. The procedure itself lasted approximately two hours, and the surgical team encountered no unexpected difficulties. 

Throughout the postoperative course, the patient received appropriate pain management with opioids and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). He was monitored closely for any signs of infection, such as fever, redness, or drainage at the surgical site. 

The patient's vital signs remained stable throughout the recovery period. He was able to tolerate a regular diet without any issues, and his bowel movements were regular. Daily wound dressings were performed, and there were no signs of wound dehiscence or infection. 

Physical therapy was initiated on postoperative day one, focusing on ambulation and deep breathing exercises. The patient showed good compliance and demonstrated marked improvement in mobilization. 

On postoperative day two, the patient complained of mild nausea, which responded well to anti-emetic medication. His pain levels were well controlled, and he was able to achieve adequate restful sleep. 

Over the next few days, the patient continued to progress well, with increasing mobility and minimal pain. He was able to discontinue the use of opioids and was transitioned to oral NSAIDs for pain relief. 

On postoperative day five, a final wound assessment was performed, and all dressing changes were discontinued. The patient was discharged home with appropriate instructions for wound care, activity restrictions, and follow-up appointments. 

The patient was counseled on signs and symptoms to watch for, including signs of infection, deep vein thrombosis, or respiratory distress. He was advised to contact his healthcare provider if any concerning symptoms or complications arose during his recovery. 

During the postoperative course, the patient did not develop any surgical complications such as infection, bleeding, or hernia recurrence. His incision site appeared to be healing well without any signs of infection. 

Overall, the patient had a successful surgical procedure without any significant complications. He tolerated the surgery well and had an uneventful postoperative course. The patient was satisfied with the outcome and expressed gratitude for the excellent care he received from the surgical team.
