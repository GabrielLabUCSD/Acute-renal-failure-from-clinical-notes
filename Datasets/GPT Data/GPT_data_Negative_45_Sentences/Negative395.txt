Patient is a 65-year-old male who has a medical history significant for hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, hyperlipidemia, and coronary artery disease. He is scheduled to undergo surgery for a hernia repair. preoperative assessment reveals that his blood pressure and blood glucose levels are well-controlled with medications.

The patient underwent coronary angiography last year, which revealed a 60% stenosis in the left anterior descending artery. He underwent a percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with placement of a drug-eluting stent at that time. Since the PCI, the patient has been on dual antiplatelet therapy with aspirin and clopidogrel.

In terms of his diabetes, the patient reports good glucose control through diet and oral hypoglycemic agents. He has been monitoring his blood glucose levels regularly and reports no episodes of hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia.

The patient's lipid profile is well-controlled with simvastatin, and his last lipid panel showed optimal levels of LDL cholesterol and total cholesterol.

He reports occasional episodes of gastroesophageal reflux disease, which are managed with lifestyle modifications and occasional use of over-the-counter antacids. No active symptoms of reflux are noted at present.

The patient denies any history of bleeding disorders or previous surgeries requiring blood transfusions. He has no known drug allergies and does not smoke or use recreational drugs.

Upon physical examination, the patient is alert and oriented, with no signs of acute distress. Vital signs are within normal limits, and he appears well-nourished. Heart and lung examination reveal no abnormalities. Abdominal examination reveals a reducible inguinal hernia.

Laboratory investigations show normal renal function, with serum creatinine and estimated glomerular filtration rate within the normal range. Urinalysis is negative for proteinuria.

Electrocardiogram shows evidence of prior inferior myocardial infarction but no acute changes. Echocardiography reveals a preserved left ventricular ejection fraction and no segmental wall motion abnormalities.

The patient's surgical risk is deemed low based on his medical history, preoperative evaluation, and lack of significant comorbidities. He is cleared for surgery with a plan for general anesthesia.

Intraoperatively, the patient tolerates the procedure well without any complications. The hernia repair is successful, and the surgical team reports no unexpected findings or difficulties.

Postoperatively, the patient is recovering in the surgical ward. Pain management is achieved with oral analgesics, and the patient reports tolerable pain levels. His vital signs remain stable, with no signs of infection or hemodynamic instability.

He is monitored for any signs of postoperative complications, such as wound infection, bleeding, or respiratory distress. However, he does not develop any complications during his hospital stay.

The patient is encouraged to engage in early mobilization and deep breathing exercises to prevent postoperative complications such as pneumonia and venous thromboembolism.

His diabetes management remains consistent, with regular blood glucose monitoring and adjustment of medications as needed. He reports no significant changes in his glucose levels and no episodes of hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia.

The patient's postoperative course is uneventful, and he is discharged home on postoperative day 3. He is provided with postoperative instructions, including wound care, restriction of heavy lifting, and follow-up appointments.

During the postoperative follow-up visit, the patient reports satisfactory healing of the surgical wound with no signs of infection. He has resumed his regular activities and is pleased with the outcome of the surgery.

In summary, the patient is a 65-year-old male with a medical history of hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, hyperlipidemia, and coronary artery disease. He underwent successful hernia repair surgery without any intraoperative or postoperative complications. His recovery was uneventful, and he was discharged home with satisfactory wound healing. The patient's diabetes management remained stable throughout his perioperative period, and he did not experience any acute medical issues during his hospital stay.
