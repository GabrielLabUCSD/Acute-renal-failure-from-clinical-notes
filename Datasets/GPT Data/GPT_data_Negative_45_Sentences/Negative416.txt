Patient is a 65-year-old male with a medical history significant for hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. He presents to the hospital for elective surgery and has been cleared by his primary care physician.

He was diagnosed with hypertension 10 years ago and has been on a stable regimen of antihypertensive medications, including lisinopril, for the past five years. His blood pressure is well controlled with this regimen, and he has not experienced any concerning symptoms related to his hypertension.

The patient was also diagnosed with hyperlipidemia six years ago and has been taking atorvastatin to manage his cholesterol levels. His last lipid panel showed a well-controlled LDL cholesterol level of 90 mg/dL.

Three years ago, he was diagnosed with type 2 diabetes mellitus and is managed with metformin 1000 mg twice daily. His glycemic control has been good, with his last HbA1c level at 6.8%. He has not experienced any diabetic complications such as peripheral neuropathy or retinopathy.

The patient has no known allergies to any medications or substances. He denies any history of smoking or alcohol use. He has a sedentary lifestyle and does not engage in regular exercise.

On physical examination, the patient weighs 180 lbs with a BMI of 28.5, indicating overweight status. He has a blood pressure of 130/80 mmHg and a regular heart rate of 70 beats per minute. Cardiovascular examination reveals no murmurs, rubs, or gallops. His lungs are clear to auscultation bilaterally. Abdominal examination is unremarkable, and there are no signs of peripheral edema or lymphadenopathy.

Lab workup reveals a normal complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel, and coagulation profile. His fasting blood glucose level is 120 mg/dL, and his HbA1c remains stable at 6.8%.

Given his stable medical conditions, the patient is deemed fit for surgery and is scheduled for a cholecystectomy due to symptomatic gallstones. Preoperative counseling is provided, and the patient is instructed to continue his antihypertensive and antidiabetic medications as prescribed.

The patient undergoes an uneventful laparoscopic cholecystectomy without any intraoperative complications. The surgical team notes that the gallbladder was significantly inflamed but no stones were left behind. The patient is transferred to the post-anesthesia care unit for monitoring.

In the immediate postoperative period, the patient recovers well from anesthesia. His vital signs remain stable, and he is comfortable after receiving appropriate pain medications. He is started on a clear liquid diet and gradually advances to a regular diet as tolerated.

Over the next few days, the patient continues to recover without any signs of surgical site infection or wound complications. He is able to walk without assistance and is encouraged to perform deep breathing exercises and use incentive spirometry to prevent complications such as pneumonia or atelectasis.

The patient's postoperative pain is well controlled with oral analgesics, and he reports minimal discomfort. He is monitored closely for signs of postoperative bleeding or bile leak, but none are observed.

On postoperative day three, the patient is successfully discharged home with instructions for wound care, limited activity for the next two weeks, and follow-up with his primary care physician in one week. He is advised to complete a course of oral antibiotics prescribed at discharge to prevent any surgical site infection.

The patient's postoperative course remains unremarkable, and he experiences no complications such as wound infection, bile leak, or respiratory complications. He recovers without any need for readmission or additional interventions.

His surgical pathology report confirms the presence of gallstones but does not identify any signs of malignancy. His postoperative appointment with the surgeon is uneventful, and he is cleared to resume his regular activities as tolerated.

In conclusion, this 65-year-old male with a medical history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus underwent an uneventful laparoscopic cholecystectomy for symptomatic gallstones. He had a smooth postoperative course without any complications, including acute renal failure. The patient was successfully discharged home and continues to recover well.
