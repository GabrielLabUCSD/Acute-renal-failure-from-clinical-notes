The patient is a 55-year-old male who has a past medical history significant for hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus.
He presents to the clinic with the complaint of increasing knee pain and a limited range of motion due to severe osteoarthritis.
The patient has tried conservative management, including physical therapy and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, but has not found relief.
After discussing the options, the patient expresses an interest in undergoing total knee replacement surgery.
He is a non-smoker and denies any allergies to medications or previous surgeries.
The patient is on a statin medication for his hyperlipidemia and takes metformin and glipizide to manage his diabetes.
He has good glucose control with a hemoglobin A1c of 6.7% and has been compliant with his medication regimen.
The patient's blood pressure is well-controlled at 130/80 mmHg on his current antihypertensive medication, lisinopril.
He denies any symptoms of angina or shortness of breath and is able to perform activities of daily living without any limitations.
The patient's physical examination reveals tenderness, swelling, and crepitus over his right knee joint.
The range of motion is significantly limited, with flexion limited to 70 degrees and extension limited to 10 degrees.
X-rays of the knee confirm severe osteoarthritis with joint space narrowing, osteophyte formation, and subchondral sclerosis.
After discussing the risks and benefits of the surgery, the patient decides to proceed with total knee replacement surgery.
He undergoes preoperative evaluation and is found to be in good overall health without any significant abnormalities on laboratory testing.
The patient is scheduled for surgery and is advised to stop taking his metformin on the morning of the procedure.
On the day of surgery, the patient arrives to the hospital and is prepared for the procedure.
Anesthesia consultation is obtained, and the patient is cleared for surgery with no concerns.
The patient is admitted to the operating room and placed under general anesthesia.
The surgery proceeds without any complications, and a new prosthetic knee joint is successfully implanted.
The patient is transferred to the recovery room, where he is monitored closely for signs of postoperative complications.
He is initially started on intravenous pain medications and is transitioned to oral pain medications as tolerated.
The patient is encouraged to start physical therapy the day after surgery and to bear weight on the operative leg with the use of crutches or a walker.
He follows up regularly with his orthopedic surgeon in the postoperative period to monitor his progress and address any concerns.
The patient's wound heals well without any signs of infection or dehiscence.
He is able to achieve a 90-degree range of motion in his knee joint by three weeks postoperative.
Physical therapy sessions help him regain strength and function in his knee, and he commits to regular exercises at home.
The patient's pain improves significantly, and he reports an improvement in his overall quality of life after the surgery.
He is slowly able to return to his usual activities, including walking, stair climbing, and playing golf.
The patient is noted to have well-controlled blood pressure, with readings consistently below 140/90 mmHg after surgery.
His lipid profile remains stable, and he continues taking his statin medication for hyperlipidemia.
No signs of uncontrolled diabetes are observed, and his latest hemoglobin A1c is reported as 6.5%.
The patient has regular follow-up visits with his primary care physician to monitor his chronic conditions and adjust medications as needed.
No postoperative complications, such as deep vein thrombosis or wound breakdown, are noted in the patient's course.
He has not experienced any acute respiratory distress or pneumonia.
The patient reports mild constipation after surgery, likely related to the combination of anesthesia and opioid pain medications.
He is advised to increase fluid intake, incorporate fiber-rich foods into his diet, and take over-the-counter stool softeners.
The constipation improves, and the patient does not require further intervention.
Overall, the patient's postoperative course has been smooth, and he is satisfied with the outcome of his knee replacement surgery.
The surgery has successfully improved his knee pain, function, and overall quality of life.
He is encouraged to continue with regular physical therapy, maintain a healthy lifestyle, and follow up with his healthcare providers for ongoing monitoring and management.
