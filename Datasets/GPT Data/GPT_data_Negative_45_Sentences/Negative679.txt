The patient is a 56-year-old male who is scheduled for surgery. His medical history is significant for hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. He has been on medication for his hypertension for the past five years and has been able to maintain his blood pressure within normal limits. His hyperlipidemia is managed with statin therapy, and his last lipid profile showed well-controlled levels. He has been diagnosed with type 2 diabetes for the past six years and has been on metformin and lifestyle modifications to manage his blood glucose. His recent HbA1c was 7.2%, indicating fair control.

The patient reports no known drug allergies. He has never smoked and consumes alcohol only occasionally. His body mass index (BMI) is 29.5, classifying him as overweight. His physical exam reveals no significant abnormalities, and his vital signs are within normal limits. Laboratory investigations, including a complete blood count, liver function tests, renal function tests, and coagulation profile, are all within normal ranges.

The patient underwent a preoperative anesthesia evaluation, and no significant concerns were identified. His airway assessment suggests no predicted difficulty with intubation. He was classified as an ASA II patient, indicating that he is a patient with mild systemic disease limiting normal activity but is otherwise healthy.

The patient's surgery is scheduled for a laparoscopic cholecystectomy due to symptomatic gallstones. He has been informed about the surgery, the risks, and the benefits, and has provided informed consent. The procedure will be performed under general anesthesia, and the patient has been instructed about the preoperative fasting period.

The surgery was uneventful, and the patient tolerated it well. During the surgery, the anesthetic team carefully monitored the patient's vital signs, including arterial blood pressure, heart rate, and oxygen saturation. He was extubated smoothly in the operating room and transferred to the post-anesthesia care unit (PACU). In the PACU, his vital signs remained stable.

His ambulation was encouraged on the first postoperative day to prevent complications such as deep vein thrombosis. The patient was able to tolerate a regular diet without any gastrointestinal discomfort. His incision site remained clean and showed no signs of infection or dehiscence.

The patient was discharged on the second postoperative day with instructions for wound care, pain management, and diet modifications. He was advised to take his prescribed medications regularly, including his antihypertensive drugs, statin, metformin, and postoperative analgesics. The importance of maintaining good glycemic control was emphasized, and the patient was educated about the signs and symptoms of hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia.

During follow-up visits, the patient's incision site continued to heal well without complications. His pain resolved gradually, allowing him to resume his normal daily activities. The patient was advised to schedule routine surveillance appointments with his primary care physician for blood pressure checks and lipid profile monitoring.

Fortunately, the patient did not experience any major complications during his postoperative course. His blood glucose levels remained within a target range, and he did not require any dose adjustments to his antidiabetic medication. No signs of infection, bleeding, or other surgical site complications were noted during the follow-up visits. The patient expressed satisfaction with the outcome of his surgery and appreciated the care provided by the healthcare team.
