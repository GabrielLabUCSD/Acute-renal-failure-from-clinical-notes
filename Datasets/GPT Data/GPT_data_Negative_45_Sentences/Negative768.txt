Patient is a 65-year-old male who is planning to have surgery for a hernia repair. He has a past medical history significant for hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. His blood pressure has been well-controlled on lisinopril 10 mg daily, and his lipid levels have been within normal limits with atorvastatin 20 mg daily. His diabetes has been managed with metformin 1000 mg twice daily, and his HbA1c has been consistently below 7%.

He is a former smoker, having quit 10 years ago, and reports occasional alcohol consumption, with no known drug allergies. He is physically active, engaging in regular cardiovascular exercise and strength training. He has not experienced any recent significant weight loss or gain.

The patient denies any symptoms of chest pain, shortness of breath, orthopnea, or lower extremity edema. His past surgical history includes an appendectomy performed 20 years ago and a tonsillectomy in childhood. He has no family history of clotting disorders or perioperative complications.

On examination, the patient is in no distress. His vital signs are within normal limits, including a blood pressure of 120/80 mmHg, heart rate of 70 beats per minute, respiratory rate of 16 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 98% on room air. His cardiovascular examination reveals regular rate and rhythm with no murmurs, gallops, or rubs. His lungs are clear to auscultation bilaterally. Abdominal examination reveals a reducible hernia in the right inguinal region, without evidence of obstruction or strangulation.

Laboratory evaluation reveals a normal complete blood count, electrolyte panel, and liver function tests. His renal function is within normal limits, with a serum creatinine level of 0.9 mg/dL. Urinalysis is negative for protein, glucose, and hematuria. A chest X-ray is performed, showing no acute pulmonary abnormalities.

The patient has been informed about the risks and benefits of the surgery, including the potential complications such as infection, bleeding, and recurrence of the hernia. He understands the need for preoperative fasting and has been instructed to take his usual medications with a sip of water on the morning of surgery.

The patient undergoes the hernia repair surgery without any intraoperative complications. Postoperatively, he is closely monitored for pain control, wound healing, and early ambulation. He receives adequate pain medication and guided incision care to minimize the risk of infection. His vital signs remain stable, and he shows normal return of bowel function. He is started on a clear liquid diet on the day of surgery and advances to a regular diet as tolerated.

The patient's postoperative course is uneventful, and he is discharged home on postoperative day 2 with prescriptions for a short course of postoperative pain medication and instructions for wound care. He is advised to follow up with his primary care physician in two weeks for a wound check and overall surgical recovery evaluation.

During the follow-up visit, the patient reports no complications related to the surgery, such as wound infection, wound dehiscence, or inability to tolerate regular diet. His incision shows signs of well-healing, with no signs of infection or delayed wound healing. The patient's pain is well-controlled with over-the-counter analgesics, and he has resumed his regular activities, including exercise.

In conclusion, the patient's preoperative course was unremarkable, and he underwent hernia repair surgery without any intraoperative complications. His postoperative course has been smooth, with no significant complications observed. The patient has recovered well and exhibits no signs of surgical site or systemic complications, allowing him to resume his regular activities.
