Patient X is a 45-year-old male with a past medical history significant for hypertension and diabetes mellitus type 2. He presents to our clinic with a planned surgery in the near future and has a pertinent medical history for the surgical procedure.

Patient X was diagnosed with hypertension 10 years ago and has been well-controlled on enalapril 10 mg daily. He also has a history of diabetes mellitus type 2, which was diagnosed 8 years ago. His diabetes is managed with metformin 1000 mg BID and he regularly monitors his blood glucose levels at home. His most recent HbA1c was 7.2%.

The patient has a known history of allergies, particularly to penicillin, and experiences a rash and itching when exposed. He also reports a remote history of smoking for 10 years, but he quit 5 years ago. He denies any history of alcohol or illicit substance use.

On physical examination, the patient's vital signs are stable with a blood pressure of 130/80 mmHg, heart rate of 75 beats per minute, and oxygen saturation of 98% on room air. His body mass index (BMI) is 28 kg/mÂ², and he appears well-nourished. Cardiopulmonary and abdominal exams are unremarkable, and there is no edema or peripheral vascular abnormalities.

Laboratory tests reveal a normal complete blood count, fasting blood glucose of 120 mg/dL, HbA1c of 7.2%, serum electrolyte levels within normal limits, and a slightly elevated creatinine level at 1.3 mg/dL. His renal function has been stable over the years, and he does not have a history of acute renal failure.

The patient is scheduled for surgery next week, and the surgical procedure being planned is a cholecystectomy for symptomatic cholelithiasis. He has been educated about the procedure, risks, and benefits and has provided informed consent.

Postoperatively, patient X is transferred to the surgical intensive care unit (ICU) for close monitoring. His anesthesia is smoothly reversed with no immediate complications. Intravenous fluids are initiated to maintain hydration, and the patient's vital signs remain stable. Pain control is achieved with a patient-controlled analgesia pump, predominantly using opioids, and his pain is well managed throughout his ICU stay.

He begins ambulating the day after surgery with assistance from the nursing staff and shows good progress in his physical mobility. The patient's nausea and vomiting are effectively managed with antiemetics, and he resumes a regular diet on postoperative day 2 without difficulty.

Patient X's wound is clean and dry, demonstrating good healing progress with no signs of infection or dehiscence. He is discharged from the ICU after 48 hours and transferred to the surgical floor for continued postoperative care.

Throughout his hospital stay, patient X does not experience any complications such as surgical site infections, uncontrolled pain, thromboembolic events, or respiratory compromise. His blood glucose levels are monitored closely, and he maintains good glycemic control with insulin sliding scale. However, he does experience postoperative constipation, which is managed with laxatives and dietary modifications.

Following his uneventful recovery in the hospital, patient X is discharged home on postoperative day 5 with appropriate pain medications and instructions to follow up in the outpatient clinic. He is advised to continue his antihypertensive and antidiabetic medications as prescribed.

Overall, patient X had a successful surgery without any major complications. His postoperative course was smooth, with no significant issues encountered.
