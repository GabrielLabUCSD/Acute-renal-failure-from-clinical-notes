The patient is a 45-year-old male who is scheduled to undergo surgery.
He has a past medical history of hypertension, diabetes mellitus, and hyperlipidemia.
His blood pressure is well controlled with a combination of antihypertensive medications, including lisinopril and hydrochlorothiazide.
He takes metformin for his diabetes.
His cholesterol levels are managed with atorvastatin.
The patient has no known drug allergies.
He does not smoke and has no history of alcohol abuse.
His surgical history includes an appendectomy and tonsillectomy in childhood.
He has experienced no previous complications or adverse reactions to anesthesia.
The patient reports no family history of surgical complications.
He presents with no current symptoms related to his chronic medical conditions.
His preoperative laboratory tests reveal normal fasting glucose levels and stable renal function.
His liver function tests, complete blood count, and coagulation profile are within normal limits.
The patient is on a well-balanced diet and exercises regularly.
He is of average height and weight for his age.
The patient is mentally and emotionally stable, displaying no signs of anxiety or depression related to the upcoming surgery.
During the surgery, the patient is monitored closely, including blood pressure, heart rate, and oxygen saturation levels.
He receives general anesthesia.
The procedure is uneventful and there are no complications during the surgery.
However, the patient develops acute renal failure following the surgery.
His urine output decreases significantly, and there is an increase in his serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen levels.
The patient complains of fatigue, feeling weak, and a decreased appetite.
He has no other signs of infection or respiratory distress.
A nephrologist is consulted and diagnoses the patient with acute tubular necrosis, the most common cause of acute renal failure following surgery.
The patient is managed with intravenous fluids to restore hydration and improve renal perfusion.
He is also prescribed medication for pain control and to manage his blood pressure.
The patient's renal function gradually improves over several days with the appropriate treatment.
He is closely monitored for any signs of complications or further deterioration in renal function.
The patient's other medical conditions are managed during his hospital stay, including regular blood glucose monitoring and medication administration.
He is discharged from the hospital once his renal function stabilizes and he is deemed medically stable.
Follow-up appointments with the nephrologist and primary care physician are scheduled to monitor the patient's renal function and adjust medications as needed.
