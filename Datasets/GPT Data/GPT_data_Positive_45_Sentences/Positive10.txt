The patient is a 65-year-old male who is scheduled for surgery. He has a significant medical history, including hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, and coronary artery disease. The patient has been taking medications for these conditions, including antihypertensives, oral hypoglycemic agents, and antiplatelet drugs. 

The patient has a history of smoking for the past 30 years, with a 20-pack year history. He has attempted to quit smoking in the past but has been unsuccessful. The patient consumes alcohol occasionally but denies any history of illicit drug use. 

On physical examination, the patient is found to be overweight, with a body mass index (BMI) of 32 kg/m2. He has central obesity and a waist circumference of 40 inches. 

The patient's preoperative laboratory investigations reveal well-controlled blood glucose levels with a fasting blood sugar of 120 mg/dL and an HbA1c of 6%. His lipid profile shows a total cholesterol of 180 mg/dL, triglycerides of 150 mg/dL, low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol of 100 mg/dL, and high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol of 45 mg/dL. His renal function tests indicate a serum creatinine of 1.0 mg/dL, with an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) of 70 mL/min/1.73m2. 

The patient's electrocardiogram (ECG) reveals sinus rhythm with occasional premature ventricular contractions (PVCs). Echocardiography shows mild left ventricular systolic dysfunction with an ejection fraction of 45%. His stress test results are negative for any inducible myocardial ischemia. 

The patient is also on aspirin therapy for secondary prevention of cardiovascular events. He is advised to stop taking aspirin seven days before the surgery to minimize the risk of bleeding complications. 

In addition to his medical conditions, the patient reports occasional episodes of dyspnea on exertion, particularly during climbing stairs or performing strenuous activities. He denies any history of chest pain, palpitations, syncope, or orthopnea. 

Given the patient's significant medical history and risk factors, the surgical team recommends performing a thorough preoperative evaluation, including a cardiology consultation, to ensure optimal management of his comorbidities during the perioperative period. 

Postoperatively, the patient develops complications in the form of acute renal failure. He presents with oliguria, which is defined as a urine output less than 400 mL per day. His renal function tests show a significant rise in serum creatinine levels to 2.5 mg/dL, with a corresponding decrease in eGFR to 30 mL/min/1.73m2. 

The patient's urine output and renal function do not improve despite conservative management, and he is diagnosed with acute tubular necrosis (ATN) as the cause of his acute renal failure. 

The surgical team initiates appropriate measures to manage the patient's acute renal failure, including maintenance of fluid and electrolyte balance, avoidance of nephrotoxic medications, and optimization of hemodynamics. 

The patient is closely monitored for signs of worsening renal function, such as increasing serum creatinine levels and declining urine output. 

The surgical team also consults a nephrologist for further management and recommendations. 

The patient undergoes daily hemodialysis sessions to remove accumulated toxins, correct fluid and electrolyte imbalances, and improve renal function. 

Despite these interventions, the patient's renal function does not recover, and he remains dialysis dependent. 

The patient's prognosis for renal recovery is guarded, considering his age and the severity of his underlying comorbidities.
