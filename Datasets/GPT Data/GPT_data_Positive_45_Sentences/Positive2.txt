The patient was started on intravenous fluids to improve his fluid status and increase urine output. Medications were administered to correct the electrolyte abnormalities and manage metabolic acidosis. Close monitoring of the patient's renal function was done throughout his hospital stay.

The cause of the patient's acute renal failure was determined to be related to intraoperative hypotension and decreased renal perfusion. The patient's blood pressure dropped significantly during the surgery, leading to inadequate blood flow to the kidneys and subsequent renal injury. Other possible causes, such as medication-related renal injury or pre-existing kidney disease, were ruled out based on the patient's medical history and the evaluation of his renal function prior to the surgery.

The patient's renal function gradually improved over the course of his hospital stay. His urine output increased, and his serum creatinine levels started to decrease. He did not require renal replacement therapy, such as dialysis, as his kidneys started to recover. The patient was closely monitored for any signs of worsening renal function or complications.

The patient's acute renal failure delayed his recovery and discharge from the hospital. He was kept under close observation and received appropriate medical care for the management of his renal condition. The patient's pain was managed with analgesics, and he was encouraged to mobilize gradually to prevent complications such as deep vein thrombosis.

As the patient's renal function continued to improve, he was discharged from the hospital with close follow-up appointments with the nephrologist.
