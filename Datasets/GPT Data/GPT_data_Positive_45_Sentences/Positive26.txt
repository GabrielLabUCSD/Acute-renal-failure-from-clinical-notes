During surgery, the patient develops an acute renal failure complication. This complication is characterized by a sudden decrease in kidney function resulting in the build-up of waste products and fluids in the body. The cause of the acute renal failure is believed to be related to intraoperative hypotension and the use of nephrotoxic medications during the surgery. Additionally, the patient received a high dose of antibiotics, which are known to have nephrotoxic effects.

To manage the acute renal failure, the patient is immediately started on intravenous fluids to help maintain adequate hydration and promote urine output. He is also closely monitored for any signs of complications, such as electrolyte imbalances or fluid overload. The surgical team consults with a nephrologist to ensure appropriate management of the acute renal failure.

The patient undergoes continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT), which involves the use of a dialysis machine to filter and remove waste products and excess fluids from the blood. This therapy helps support kidney function and prevent further complications associated with renal failure, such as electrolyte imbalances and acid-base disturbances.

The patient's condition gradually improves over the next few days, with his urine output gradually increasing and his blood test results showing a decline in creatinine and BUN levels. The CRRT is gradually tapered off, and the patient's kidney function recovers to near-normal levels. He is scheduled for regular follow-up appointments with the nephrologist to monitor his kidney function and ensure a complete recovery.

As part of the long-term management plan, the patient is advised to avoid nephrotoxic medications, such as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and certain antibiotics, unless absolutely necessary. He is also encouraged to maintain a healthy lifestyle, including regular exercise and a balanced diet, to minimize the risk of future kidney complications.
