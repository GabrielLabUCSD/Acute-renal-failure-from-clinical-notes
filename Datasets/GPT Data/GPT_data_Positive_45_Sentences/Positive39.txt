He has a family history of heart disease, with his father and two siblings having had heart attacks. The patient has been taking medications to manage his hypertension and diabetes, including lisinopril and metformin.

In terms of his surgical history, the patient has had a previous cholecystectomy due to gallstones, as well as a hernia repair surgery. He tolerated both surgeries well and did not experience any complications or adverse reactions to anesthesia.

The patient is a non-smoker and consumes alcohol in moderation. He has been following a healthy diet and exercises regularly, with a focus on aerobic exercises such as walking and swimming. His most recent laboratory results indicate a well-controlled blood pressure and blood sugar levels within the normal range.

However, the patient developed acute renal failure as a complication after the surgery. The exact cause of the renal failure is currently under investigation, but it is suspected to be related to the use of certain medications during the surgical procedure. The patient's renal function worsened in the post-operative period, with an increase in serum creatinine levels. He also developed oliguria, with a decreased urine output.

The patient's vital signs remained stable, with blood pressure within the normal range and heart rate within normal limits. However, his urine output was significantly decreased, and he exhibited signs of fluid overload, including edema and shortness of breath.

A renal ultrasound was performed, revealing no structural abnormalities in the kidneys. Additionally, a urine analysis showed no signs of infection or other abnormalities. The patient's electrolyte levels were also monitored, and he was started on diuretic therapy to promote urine output and reduce fluid overload.

The patient's renal function gradually improved over the course of several days, with a gradual decrease in serum creatinine levels. His urine output also increased, indicating improved renal function. The diuretic therapy was gradually tapered off as the patient's fluid overload resolved.

Throughout his hospital stay, the patient was closely monitored by a multidisciplinary team, including nephrologists and surgeons. His medications were adjusted to minimize their impact on renal function. Additionally, dietary modifications were made to ensure a low-sodium and low-protein diet, which would not further strain the kidneys.

As the patient's renal function improved, his laboratory results returned to within normal ranges. He was discharged from the hospital after ten days with appropriate follow-up care arranged, including regular monitoring of renal function and close communication with his primary care physician.

The patient was advised to continue his routine medications for hypertension and diabetes. He was also counseled on lifestyle modifications to reduce his risk factors for future complications, including maintaining a healthy weight, adhering to a low-sodium diet, and engaging in regular exercise. He was also educated on the importance of close monitoring of renal function and reporting any concerning symptoms.
