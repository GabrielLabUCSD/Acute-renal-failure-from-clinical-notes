He underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) and received stents in his coronary arteries. The patient's cardiologist has conducted regular follow-ups and has deemed him fit for surgery based on his stable cardiac status. Mr. Smith has no symptoms of angina or heart failure at present.

He was diagnosed five years ago and has been managed with bronchodilators and inhaled corticosteroids. His lung function tests indicate mild to moderate airflow limitation, and he occasionally experiences shortness of breath with exertion.

It is worth noting that Mr. Smith is a current smoker, which increases his risk for surgical complications, such as poor wound healing and respiratory complications. The surgical team will provide him with smoking cessation counseling to improve his chances of a successful surgery and recovery.

Another important aspect of Mr. Smith's medical history is his history of alcohol misuse. He has a past history of heavy alcohol consumption but has significantly reduced his intake over the past two years. Nonetheless, his alcohol consumption could still impact his liver function and potentially affect anesthesia and medication metabolism during surgery.

Postoperatively, Mr. Smith is at risk of developing complications such as acute renal failure (ARF) due to his age, preexisting conditions, and the surgical stress. His baseline kidney function will be assessed before surgery to establish a reference point for any subsequent changes.

During surgery, the patient will be under general anesthesia, and measures will be taken to prevent hypotension and maintain adequate renal perfusion. However, despite these precautions, there is always a risk of developing acute renal failure, especially in patients with multiple comorbidities.

If Mr. Smith develops ARF after surgery, it would most likely manifest as an abrupt decrease in urine output, elevated serum creatinine levels, and fluid and electrolyte imbalances. The surgical team will closely monitor his renal function and manage any complications accordingly.

Treatment for postoperative acute renal failure may involve fluid and electrolyte management, diuretics, and renal replacement therapy if necessary. The goal will be to restore and maintain adequate renal function while addressing the underlying cause of the renal failure.
