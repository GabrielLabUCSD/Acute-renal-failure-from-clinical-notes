Patient Summary:

The patient is a 60-year-old male who is planning to undergo surgery for an undisclosed reason. The patient has a complex medical history, including hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. He has had these conditions for the past 15 years and is currently on multiple medications to manage them, including metoprolol for hypertension, statins for hyperlipidemia, and metformin for diabetes.

In addition to his chronic conditions, the patient has a history of myocardial infarction, which occurred 5 years ago. He underwent percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) and received two drug-eluting stents in the left anterior descending artery. Since then, the patient has been on dual antiplatelet therapy with aspirin and clopidogrel.

The patient also has a history of chronic kidney disease (stage 3), which was diagnosed 10 years ago. He has been monitored regularly by a nephrologist and has been advised to maintain good hydration and avoid nephrotoxic medications. Despite these measures, his renal function has gradually declined over time.

Possible contributing factors include hypoperfusion during the surgery, exposure to nephrotoxic agents, and the patient's underlying chronic kidney disease.

The patient's postoperative course was complicated by oliguria, with urine output averaging around 200 mL per day. Laboratory tests revealed elevated serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen levels, indicating impaired renal function. The patient also developed electrolyte imbalances, including hyperkalemia, requiring urgent treatment with intravenous fluids and diuretics.

Given the patient's history of myocardial infarction and the need for dual antiplatelet therapy, a multidisciplinary team was consulted to determine the management of the patient's acute renal failure. It was decided to continue the antiplatelet therapy to prevent thromboembolic events, while close monitoring of renal function and electrolytes was instituted.

The patient's renal function gradually improved over the course of several weeks. His urine output increased, and his serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen levels normalized. The hyperkalemia was also successfully managed with medical intervention.

During the recovery period, the patient was counseled on the importance of continuing to manage his chronic conditions, including hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and diabetes mellitus. He was advised to follow a heart-healthy diet, exercise regularly, and adhere to his medication regimen.

The patient's follow-up appointments included regular visits to the nephrologist to monitor his renal function. He was also referred to a cardiologist to assess the need for any additional cardiac interventions in light of his acute renal failure.
