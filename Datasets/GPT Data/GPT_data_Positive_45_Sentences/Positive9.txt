Unfortunately, the patient develops acute renal failure (ARF) after surgery. The probable cause of ARF is acute tubular necrosis, which can occur due to various factors such as hypotension during surgery, exposure to nephrotoxic medications, or sepsis. This condition is characterized by an abrupt decline in renal function, resulting in decreased urine output and elevated serum creatinine levels.

The patient's medical team immediately initiates treatment for ARF, which includes fluid resuscitation to optimize circulating blood volume, discontinuing any potentially nephrotoxic medications, and close monitoring of his electrolytes and fluid status. In severe cases, renal replacement therapy may be required to support the patient's kidney function. The healthcare team will also address the underlying cause of ARF to prevent further progression and complications.

In addition to ARF, the patient may experience other postoperative complications such as wound infection, pneumonia, or thromboembolic events. To prevent these complications, the healthcare team will implement measures such as prophylactic antibiotics, early ambulation, incentive spirometry, and sequential compression devices.

The patient's recovery from ARF will depend on the underlying cause, the extent of kidney damage, and the effectiveness of treatment. It is crucial for the healthcare team to monitor his renal function closely and adjust the treatment plan accordingly. With appropriate management and supportive care, the patient has a good chance of recovering from ARF and achieving optimal renal function.
