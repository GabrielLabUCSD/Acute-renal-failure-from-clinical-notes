Patient Summary: 

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and left leg radiculopathy. 

The patient has a history of smoking but has since quit. The patient has high blood pressure and hyperlipidemia.

The patient had physical therapy and epidural steroid injections without success.

The patient was taking NSAIDs and muscle relaxants for pain management.

The patient opted to undergo surgery for a lumbar fusion.

Intraoperatively, the patient had stable vital signs and there were no complications.

Postoperatively, the patient reported significant pain relief and improved mobility.

On the first postoperative day, the patient complained of nausea and vomiting, which was managed with antiemetics.

The patient was discharged home on postoperative day two with a baseline level of pain and function. 

Following discharge, the patient reported some increased pain and limited mobility but was overall improving. 

The patient was taking postoperative pain medication as prescribed. 

One week postoperative, the patient reported a significant increase in pain and was unable to ambulate.

Upon further examination, the patient was found to have a wound infection at the incision site. 

The patient was immediately taken to the operating room for debridement and wound cleaning. 

The patient remained in the hospital for an additional 5 days for antibiotic treatment.

The patient tolerated the treatment without complications and was eventually discharged home with a wound vac.

The patient was advised to take pain medication as needed and to gradually increase his activity level over the next several weeks.

One month post-op, the patient reported mild pain and was able to walk independently. 

Follow-up imaging showed satisfactory bony fusion and the patient was cleared to resume full activity without restrictions.
