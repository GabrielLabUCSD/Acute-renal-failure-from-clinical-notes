Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic back pain and sciatica. The patient has a history of smoking and alcohol use. The patient has tried physical therapy, pain medication, and injections without relief. The patient is now considering surgery as a last resort.

Preoperatively, the patient's vital signs were normal. The patient had a thorough workup, including blood work and imaging studies, which revealed no significant abnormalities. The patient's surgical history includes a cholecystectomy 10 years ago.

Intraoperatively, the patient had a lumbar fusion with instrumentation. The surgery was uneventful, and the patient tolerated it well.

Postoperatively, the patient had a complicated course. On the first night, the patient had a fever and increased pain. The patient was monitored closely, and the fever resolved with antibiotics and pain management. On the second night, the patient had a sudden onset of numbness and weakness in the lower extremities. This was concerning for a neurological deficit, and the patient was immediately taken back to the operating room for an emergency decompression. The patient was found to have a hematoma that was compressing the nerves, which was evacuated successfully. The patient had a prolonged hospital stay as a result.

The patient was discharged home on postoperative day 10 with a successful outcome and minimal residual pain. Follow-up appointments were scheduled with the surgeon and pain management team.

Complications:

* Postoperative fever
* Increased pain
* Neurological deficit (numbness and weakness in the lower extremities)
* Hematoma compressing the nerves

The patient did not experience acute renal failure as a complication.
