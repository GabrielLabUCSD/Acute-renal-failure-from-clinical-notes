Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presented with a history of chronic back pain and muscle weakness. He has been diagnosed with degenerative disc disease and spinal stenosis. The patient has tried conservative management, including physical therapy and medication, without significant relief. He is scheduled to undergo a lumbar fusion surgery to alleviate his symptoms.

The patient has no significant medical history, including no history of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, or respiratory disease. He is currently taking no medications. He denies any history of smoking or substance abuse.

The patient's surgical history includes a cholecystectomy 10 years ago without any complications. He has no known allergies to medications or anesthesia. He is currently working full-time as an office worker and is eager to return to work as soon as possible after surgery.

The patient understands the risks and benefits of surgery, including the potential for infection, nerve damage, and blood clots. He has been instructed to stop taking any non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) for one week before surgery.

Postoperative Course:
The patient had an uneventful surgery and was taken to the recovery room in stable condition. He experienced some postoperative nausea and vomiting, which were well-controlled with medication. He had some mild pain, which was managed with analgesics.

The patient was able to return to work four weeks after surgery, as planned. He was advised to avoid heavy lifting and bending for six weeks. He reported some mild numbness in his legs, which resolved within two weeks.

Complications:
The patient did not experience any complications during his surgery or postoperative course.

In conclusion, this patient with degenerative disc disease and spinal stenosis underwent a lumbar fusion surgery and had an uneventful postoperative course. He was able to return to work four weeks later without any significant complications.
