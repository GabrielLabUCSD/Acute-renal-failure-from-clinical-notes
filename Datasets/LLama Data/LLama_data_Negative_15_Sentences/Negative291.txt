Patient Summary:

The patient is a 55-year-old female who presents with a history of chronic back pain and decreased mobility. She has tried conservative treatments, including physical therapy and medication, without significant relief. The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, and she quit smoking 10 years ago. She denies any recent weight loss or night sweats.

The patient's surgical history includes a cholecystectomy and a hysterectomy. She has no history of cardiovascular disease or pulmonary disease. She has a positive family history of breast cancer and colon cancer.

Preoperative lab work reveals a normal complete blood count, electrolyte panel, and liver function tests. Her creatinine level is 1.2 mg/dL. She has a high BMI, at 35 kg/m2.

The patient underwent a lumbar fusion surgery. The procedure was uncomplicated, and the patient had a smooth extubation. She was transferred to the post-anesthesia care unit (PACU) for recovery.

During the postoperative period, the patient's pain was well-controlled with narcotic medication. She experienced some nausea and vomiting on the first postoperative day but responded well to antiemetics.

On the second postoperative day, the patient developed a fever of 101.3°F (38.5°C). Blood cultures were drawn, and the patient was started on broad-spectrum antibiotics. The fever resolved within 24 hours, and the patient was afebrile for the remainder of the hospital stay.

The patient's wound healed uneventfully, and she was discharged home on postoperative day 7. She was advised to follow up with her surgeon in 2 weeks for suture removal and to continue physical therapy for back pain rehabilitation.

Overall, the patient had a complex postoperative course, with a brief episode of fever that was effectively managed with antibiotics. She recovered well and was discharged home without any complications.
