The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and left leg radiculopathy. The patient has been diagnosed with a lumbar disc herniation and has tried conservative management, including physical therapy and medication, without significant relief. The patient is a candidate for lumbar fusion surgery.

The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, and is currently taking medication for both conditions. The patient has no known allergies to medications or latex.

The patient has a history of smoking but has quit within the past year. The patient drinks alcohol occasionally but denies any significant alcohol use disorder.

The patient has a history of substance abuse and has been sober for the past 5 years.

The patient has no history of previous surgeries or significant medical complications.

The patient"s physical examination shows limited mobility in the lower back and left leg, with positive findings for left L5 radiculopathy.

The patient"s MRI shows a large L4-L5 disc herniation with significant nerve root compression.

The patient underwent lumbar fusion surgery without any complications.

The patient had a postoperative course that was remarkable for some expected discomfort and limited mobility.

The patient was discharged home on postoperative day 3 with a prescription for pain medication and instructions for wound care and physical therapy.

The patient followed up with the surgeon at two weeks and reported significant improvement in his pain and mobility.

The patient was able to return to work at eight weeks postoperative without any restrictions.

The patient"s one-year follow-up showed sustained improvement in his symptoms and no complications or recurrences.
