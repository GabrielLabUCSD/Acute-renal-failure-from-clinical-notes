Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male with a history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and obesity. He has been a smoker for the past 10 years and has a family history of cardiovascular disease. The patient has been experiencing worsening left knee pain for the past year, which has limited his mobility and daily activities. Physical examination reveals a swollen and tender left knee with a reduced range of motion. Radiological findings show severe osteoarthritis with bone-on-bone contact and a large osteophyte. The patient has been taking over-the-counter pain medication without significant relief.

The patient underwent a left total knee arthroplasty (TKA) procedure under general anesthesia. The surgery was uneventful, and the patient was transferred to the post-anesthesia care unit for recovery. He experienced some postoperative nausea and vomiting, which was managed with antiemetics. The patient's pain was well-controlled with opioids, and he was able to ambulate with assistance on the first postoperative day.

The patient continued to make good progress during his hospital course, and he was discharged home on postoperative day 3 with a walker and a pain pump. He was advised to follow a rehabilitation program that included physical therapy and home exercises to regain strength and mobility in his knee.

During the follow-up appointment at 2 weeks, the patient reported some mild pain and swelling in his knee, which was managed with ice application and elevation. He was able to walk independently without assistance and had improved range of motion. At 6 weeks, the patient reported significant improvement in his knee pain and function, and he was able to resume his normal activities, including driving and light exercises.

However, at 3 months, the patient presented with a 2-day history of worsening left knee pain, swelling, and erythema. Physical examination revealed a warm and tender knee with a reduced range of motion. The patient was diagnosed with a superficial wound infection and was treated with oral antibiotics. The infection resolved completely within 2 weeks, and the patient was able to continue his rehabilitation program without any further complications.

At 6 months, the patient reported significant improvement in his knee pain and function, and he was able to perform all his daily activities without assistance. He was able to climb stairs and resume his recreational activities, including golf and cycling. The patient was satisfied with the outcome of his surgery and had a positive attitude towards his future health.
