Patient Summary:

The patient is a 55-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and left leg radiculopathy. He has tried conservative management, including physical therapy and medication, without significant relief. The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, and he is a former smoker. He denies any recent weight loss or fever.

On examination, the patient has decreased sensation in his left L5 and S1 dermatomes, and positive straight leg raise on the left. His gait is antalgic, and he has difficulty ambulating without assistance.

Imaging studies, including MRI and CT scans, reveal a large L4-L5 disc herniation with compression of the left L5 nerve root. The patient is scheduled for an L4-L5 discectomy.

The patient's surgical history includes a cholecystectomy and a hernia repair. He has no allergies to medications or anesthesia.

The patient's postoperative course is significant for a prolonged stay in the recovery room, as he experienced nausea and vomiting after anesthesia. However, he recovered quickly and was able to go home the next day.

One week after surgery, the patient developed a wound infection, which was treated with antibiotics. He also experienced some numbness in his left foot, which resolved over time.

At four weeks postoperatively, the patient reported significant improvement in his back pain and was able to ambulate without assistance. He returned to work at six weeks postoperatively.

At six months follow-up, the patient reported continued improvement in his back pain and was able to resume his normal activities, including golf and hiking.

Complications: Wound infection, numbness in left foot.

Conclusion: The patient underwent an L4-L5 discectomy for chronic lower back pain and left leg radiculopathy. His postoperative course was significant for a prolonged stay in the recovery room, a wound infection, and numbness in his left foot. However, he experienced significant improvement in his back pain and was able to resume his normal activities at six months follow-up.
