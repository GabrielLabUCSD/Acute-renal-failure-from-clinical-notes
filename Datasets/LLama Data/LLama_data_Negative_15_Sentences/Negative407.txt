Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and left leg radiculopathy. He has been diagnosed with spondylolisthesis and spinal stenosis. He has tried conservative management including physical therapy and medications without significant relief. He is a candidate for lumbar decompression surgery.

The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, which are well-controlled with medication. He is a non-smoker and denies any significant allergy or adverse reactions to anesthesia. He has a history ofprevious abdominal surgery for gallbladder removal.

On examination, the patient has a decreased sensation in his left lower extremity and positive straight leg raise test. His lumbar spine shows signs of degenerative changes on imaging studies.

The patient underwent lumbar decompression surgery under general anesthesia. The procedure involved diskectomy and fusion of lumbar vertebrae. The patient had an uneventful postoperative course and was able to ambulate independently on the first postoperative day.

The patient was discharged home on postoperative day 3 with instructions to continue his usual medications and to gradually increase his activity level. He followed up with his surgeon and primary care physician regularly and had no complications during the postoperative period.

The patient reported mild soreness at the incision site, which resolved within a week. He also experienced temporary leg weakness, which improved gradually over the next few weeks.

At his 6-month follow-up appointment, the patient reported significant improvement in his back pain and left leg radiculopathy. He was able to resume his normal activities, including working and exercising.
