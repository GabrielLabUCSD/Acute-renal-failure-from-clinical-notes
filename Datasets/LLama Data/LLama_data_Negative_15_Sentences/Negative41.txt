Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic back pain and Bilateral knee pain. The patient has a history of Hypertension and Hyperlipidemia. The patient had a previous left shoulder surgery 10 years ago. The patient is currently taking ibuprofen and tramadol for pain management. 

The patient underwent a left total knee replacement surgery 2 weeks ago. The patient"s postoperative course was uneventful, with manageable pain and no complications. The patient was discharged on postoperative day 3 with a prescription for oxycodone and instructions to continue tramadol. 

The patient experienced some nausea and vomiting on postoperative day 2 and 3, which was managed with ondansetron. The patient also had some mild constipation which was managed with colace and Miralax.

The patient was instructed to follow up with the surgeon in 2 weeks and physical therapy was initiated on postoperative day 5.

The patient reported increased knee pain and swelling on postoperative day 7, for which he was prescribed additional pain medication by the surgeon.

The patient was readmitted to the hospital on postoperative day 10 due to worsening knee pain, swelling, and redness. The patient was found to have an elevated white blood cell count and a positive CRP. The patient was diagnosed with a probable knee joint infection. 

The patient underwent an urgent knee aspiration, which revealed a purulent effusion. The patient was started on IV antibiotics and underwent a washout of the knee joint. The patient had a significant improvement in pain and swelling after the procedure.

The patient remained in the hospital for a further 5 days for IV antibiotics and close monitoring. The patient was discharged home with a prescription for oral antibiotics and instructions for wound care and physical therapy.

The patient had a follow-up appointment with the surgeon 2 weeks after discharge, and knee aspiration was repeated, which showed no signs of infection. The patient continued to improve and had a full range of motion and minimal pain at the 6-week mark.
