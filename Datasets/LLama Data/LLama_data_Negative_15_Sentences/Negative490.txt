Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and left leg radiculopathy. He has tried conservative management including physical therapy and medication without significant relief. He has a history of smoking but quit 2 years ago. His medical history includes hypertension and hyperlipidemia, which are well-controlled with medication. He denies any history of previous surgery or allergies. 

The patient underwent a lumbar decompression and fusion surgery. The procedure was performed under general anesthesia and took approximately 3 hours. He had an uneventful postoperative course and was able to ambulate independently within the first 24 hours. He experienced some postoperative nausea and vomiting but was successfully treated with antiemetics. He had some mild wound discomfort but did not require any narcotic medication. 

On postoperative day 2, the patient reported some urinary retention and was treated with a foley catheter. He was discharged home on postoperative day 3 with a wound drain in place. He attended follow-up appointments regularly and had no signs of wound infection or neuropraxia. He was able to return to work full-time at 6 weeks and resume driving at 8 weeks postoperatively. 

He had some mild left lower extremity weakness and numbness at his 6-week follow-up appointment but demonstrated significant overall improvement in his pain and functional status. At his 12-week follow-up appointment, he reported complete resolution of his left leg radiculopathy and was able to ambulate without any assistive devices. 

Overall, the patient had a successful postsurgical course and experienced significant improvement in his symptoms. He was able to return to work and resume his normal activities without any significant complications.
