Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and sciatica. He has tried various conservative treatments, including physical therapy and medication, without significant relief. The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, and he is a habitual smoker. He denies any previous surgeries or significant medical history.

The patient was scheduled for a lumbar fusion surgery. The surgery went smoothly, and the patient was extubated in the operating room. He was transferred to the post-anesthesia care unit (PACU) for recovery.

In the PACU, the patient experienced some nausea and vomiting, which was managed with antiemetics. He also had some discomfort and pain, which was managed with patient-controlled analgesia (PCA). The patient remained in the PACU for several hours before being transferred to the surgical floor.

On the surgical floor, the patient continued to recover from the anesthesia and surgery. He was monitored closely for any complications and received regular pain management. The patient was able to ambulate and move around with assistance.

On postoperative day 2, the patient developed a fever and tachycardia. Blood cultures were drawn, and the patient was started on broad-spectrum antibiotics. The fever resolved, and the patient continued to recover.

On postoperative day 3, the patient reported increased pain in his lower back. The patient was evaluated by the surgical team, and it was determined that the pain was due to a minor wound complication. The patient received additional pain management, and the complication resolved.

The patient remained in the hospital for several more days, recovering from the surgery and receiving physical therapy to regain strength and mobility. He was discharged home with a prescription for pain medication and instructions for wound care.

Following discharge, the patient experienced some constipation, which was managed with laxatives. He also had some mild urinary retention, which resolved with bladder training.

The patient followed up with his surgeon and primary care physician, and he was seen in the clinic several times for wound checks and to monitor his recovery. He has been doing well and has had no significant complications.

Overall, the patient had a successful surgery and postoperative course, with only minor complications that were effectively managed. He has been recovering well and is expected to make a full recovery.
