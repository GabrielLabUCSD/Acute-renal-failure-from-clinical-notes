Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents for elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy. He has a history of chronic gallstones and symptomatic cholelithiasis, with persistent right upper quadrant pain and nausea. The patient has no allergies and is taking no medications. He has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, but both conditions are well-controlled on medication. The patient is a current smoker and drinks alcohol socially.

Preoperative evaluation reveals a normal ECG and chest x-ray. Laboratory tests show a slight elevation in liver enzymes, but otherwise normal results. The patient is at low risk for postoperative complications and is a good candidate for laparoscopic surgery.

Intraoperatively, the patienttolerates the procedure well and there are no significant difficulties or complications. The gallbladder is removed successfully and the patient is transferred to the recovery room in stable condition.

Postoperatively, the patient experiences some discomfort and pain, but this is well-managed with analgesics. He is able to tolerate a clear liquid diet and is discharged home the following day.

During the postoperative period, the patient experiences some nausea and vomiting, but this is managed with antiemetics. He is able to return to his normal activities within a week and is seen for follow-up in the clinic 2 weeks after discharge. At this time, the patient is doing well and has no complaints or concerns.

Overall, the patient had an uneventful postoperative course and did not experience any significant complications. His recovery was uncomplicated and he was able to return to his normal activities without any issues.
