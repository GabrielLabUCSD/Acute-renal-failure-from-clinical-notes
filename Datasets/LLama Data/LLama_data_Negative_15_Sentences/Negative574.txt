Patient Summary: 

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents for elective surgery. He has a medical history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and non-insulin dependent diabetes mellitus (NIDDM). His surgical history includes a cholecystectomy 10 years ago. He denies any allergies to medications or anesthesia. He is currently taking atorvastatin, lisinopril, and metformin. He had a colonoscopy 5 years ago that showed benign polyps. He has been a heavy smoker for the past 20 years but quit 5 years ago. He drinks alcohol occasionally. He has no history of cardiovascular disease, lung disease, or neurological disorders. His family history is significant for coronary artery disease in his father. He is employed as an office worker and has a high school education. He is married and has two children. He denies any recent travel or exposure to illnesses.

Postoperative Course: 
After surgery, the patient had a difficult time recovering from anesthesia. He experienced nausea, vomiting, and dizziness for several hours. He also had a significant amount of pain, for which he received intravenous morphine. His blood pressure was elevated during the first 24 hours, but it was controlled with medication. He had a nasogastric tube inserted for 24 hours to help with nausea and vomiting. He was able to start oral medications 24 hours after surgery and was tolerating a clear liquid diet. He was discharged from the hospital on postoperative day 3 without any complications.

Complications:
The patient did not experience any complications during his hospital stay or after discharge. He had a smooth and uneventful recovery and was able to return to work within 2 weeks of surgery. Follow-up appointments with his surgeon and primary care physician showed that his wounds were healing properly and he was not experiencing any pain or discomfort.
