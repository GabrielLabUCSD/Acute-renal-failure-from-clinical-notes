Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a medical history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and obesity. He has been experiencing worsening left knee pain for the past year, which has been diagnosed as osteoarthritis. The patient has tried conservative management, including physical therapy and medication, but has not experienced sufficient relief. He is planning to undergo a total knee arthroplasty.

The patient's surgical history includes a cholecystectomy and a hernia repair. He has no known allergies and is not taking any medications that may interact with anesthesia. He is a current smoker and drinks alcohol occasionally.

The patient's family medical history is significant for his father's history of coronary artery disease and his mother's history of breast cancer. He has two adult children who are healthy.

The patient's social history reveals that he works as a desk clerk and has a high school education. He lives alone and has a support system of friends and family.

The patient's review of systems is positive for left knee pain, stiffness, and swelling. He also experiences occasional shortness of breath and fatigue.

The patient's physical examination reveals a BMI of 35 and a markedly swollen and tender left knee. His range of motion is limited, and he has a noticeable limp.

The patient's laboratory results are within normal limits, except for a mildly elevated blood urea nitrogen (BUN) and creatinine. His electrocardiogram (ECG) shows a normal sinus rhythm.

The patient's imaging studies, including X-rays and a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), confirm the diagnosis of osteoarthritis and show significant joint space narrowing and bone spurring.

The patient's postoperative course is uneventful, and he is discharged home on postoperative day 3. He experiences some pain and discomfort, which is managed with medication. He follows up with his surgeon and physical therapist as directed.

At his 6-week follow-up appointment, the patient reports improved pain and increased mobility. His wound is healing well, and he is progressing with physical therapy.

At his 3-month follow-up appointment, the patient reports continued improvement in his symptoms and is able to ambulate without assistance. He has returned to work and resumed his normal activities.

There are no complications during the patient's postoperative course.
