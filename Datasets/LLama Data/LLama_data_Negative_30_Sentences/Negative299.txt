Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic back pain and left leg radiculopathy. He has been diagnosed with a lumbar herniated disc and spinal stenosis. The patient has tried physical therapy and medication but has not experienced significant relief. He is a candidate for lumbar decompression surgery.

The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, which are well-controlled with medication. He is a non-smoker and denies any alcohol or illicit drug use. He works as a software engineer and is eager to return to work as soon as possible after surgery.

On the day of surgery, the patient is taken to the operating room and placed under general anesthesia. The procedure is uneventful, and the patient is extubated and transferred to the recovery room in stable condition.

Postoperatively, the patient experiences some expected discomfort, nausea, and vomiting. He is managed with pain medication and antiemetics. He is encouraged to ambulate and perform deep breathing exercises to reduce the risk of respiratory complications.

On the first postoperative day, the patient develops a fever of 100.4 degrees Fahrenheit. He is started on broad-spectrum antibiotics for possible surgical site infection. Blood cultures are sent, and the patient is closely monitored.

On the second postoperative day, the patient's fever resolves, and he is able to tolerate oral medications. He is started on a liquid diet and progresses to a soft food diet as tolerated.

On the third postoperative day, the patient experiences some worsening of pain, which is managed with a increase in pain medication. He is encouraged to continue with deep breathing exercises and ambulation.

On the fourth postoperative day, the patient is transferred to the surgical ward for further recovery. He is able to ambulate independently and is Tolerating a regular diet. He is discharged home on the fifth postoperative day with a follow-up appointment in one week.

Throughout the postoperative course, the patient is closely monitored for signs of neurological deficits, infection, or other complications. He is encouraged to follow a gentle exercise program and to progress his activities gradually.

The patient experiences a smooth recovery and is able to return to work in six weeks. He follows up with his surgeon regularly and reports significant improvement in his symptoms.
