Patient Summary:

The patient is a 55-year-old male who presented for a preoperative evaluation for an elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy. The patient has a history of gallstones and chronic gallbladder inflammation, with symptoms of abdominal pain and nausea. The patient has no allergies and takes no medications.

The patient's medical history includes hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and non-insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus. The patient had a myocardial infarction 20 years ago and has been compliant with medications and lifestyle modifications since then. The patient also has a history of pulmonary embolism and takes warfarin for anticoagulation.

The patient has tried conservative management for his gallbladder disease, including lifestyle modifications and medications, but has not had relief of symptoms. The patient has no history of previous abdominal surgery and has never had a blood transfusion.

The patient's family history includes a mother who died of breast cancer at age 55. The patient has one sibling who is healthy.

On physical examination, the patient's vital signs are normal except for a slightly elevated blood pressure. The patient has a healthy-appearing abdomen with no visible scars. The patient has normal lung fields and no pedal edema.

The patient's laboratory results show an elevated white blood cell count and a moderate elevation in alanine transaminase and aspartate transaminase. The patient's INR is within the therapeutic range.

The patient underwent a laparoscopic cholecystectomy without any complications. The patient's postoperative course was unremarkable, with no nausea or vomiting. The patient was discharged on the second postoperative day with instructions to follow a low-fat diet and to take ibuprofen for pain management as needed.

One week after discharge, the patient presented to the emergency department with worsening abdominal pain and fever. The patient was found to have a postoperative wound infection and was admitted for further management. The patient was treated with antibiotics and underwent a course of wound care. The patient's fever and pain resolved, and the patient was discharged after a 10-day hospitalization.

One month after discharge, the patient presented to the office for a follow-up appointment. The patient's incision site was well-healed, and the patient reported minimal abdominal discomfort. The patient was advised to continue taking a low-fat diet and to consider a follow-up appointment with a gastroenterologist to evaluate for any residual symptoms.

- Wound infection

No acute renal failure was present in this patient's postoperative course.
