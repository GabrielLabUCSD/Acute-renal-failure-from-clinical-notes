Patient Summary:

The patient is a 55-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic back pain and decreased mobility. He has a history of smoking and alcohol use. He has tried various conservative treatments, including physical therapy and medication, without significant relief. He was diagnosed with lumbar spinal stenosis and chronic degenerative disc disease. He underwent a lumbar decompression and fusion surgery.

Postoperative Course:
The patient had a successful surgery and was discharged from the hospital on the third postoperative day. He was prescribed pain medication and physical therapy to aid in his recovery. He followed up with his surgeon one week after discharge and reported significant improvement in his pain and mobility.

Complications:
The patient did not experience any acute complications during his postoperative course. However, he did develop a seroma at the surgical site, which resolved on its own within a week. He also experienced some numbness and tingling in his legs, which improved over time with physical therapy.

Follow-up:
The patient had a follow-up appointment with his surgeon six weeks after surgery. At that time, he reported continued improvement in his symptoms and was able to ambulate without assistance. He was advised to continue with physical therapy and gradual return to his normal activities.

Overall, the patient had a successful surgery and postoperative course. He experienced some minor complications, but they resolved without any long-term effects. He was able to return to his normal activities and reported significant improvement in his quality of life.
