Patient Summary:

This patient, a 45-year-old male, presents with a history of chronic back pain and left leg radiculopathy. He has tried physical therapy and medication without significant relief. Imaging studies show a herniated disc at L4-L5. He decides to undergo a lumbar discectomy.

Preoperative:

* The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia.
* He takes aspirin and metformin.
* He is allergic to penicillin.
* He has a history of smoking but quit 5 years ago.
* He drinks alcohol occasionally.

Intraoperative:

* The patient undergoes general anesthesia.
* The surgery is performed via a posterior approach.
* The disc is completely removed.
* There are no intraoperative complications.

Postoperative:

* The patient is transferred to the PACU for recovery.
* He experiences some postoperative pain but is managed with IV Tylenol and fentanyl.
* He has a wound drain that is removed on postoperative day 2.
* He is ambulatory on postoperative day 1.
* He is discharged home on postoperative day 3.

Complications:

* He experiences some nausea and vomiting postoperatively but improves with antiemetics.
* He develops a wound infection that is treated with antibiotics.
* He has some urinary retention that resolves with catheterization.
* He has some mild bilateral neuropraxia that resolves within 2 weeks.

Follow-up:

* The patient follows up with his surgeon at 2 weeks and 6 weeks postoperatively.
* He experiences ongoing improvement in his back pain and radiculopathy.
* There are no further complications.

Overall, this patient had a successful lumbar discectomy with a brief postoperative course and minimal complications. He experiences ongoing improvement in his symptoms during follow-up.
