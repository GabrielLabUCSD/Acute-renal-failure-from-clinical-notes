Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a medical history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and obesity. He has a history of smoking but quit 5 years ago. He denies any significant previous surgical history.

Currently, the patient is scheduled for an elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy to treat symptomatic gallstones. He has been experiencing intermittent right upper quadrant abdominal pain and nausea for the past year.

The patient's physical examination reveals a temperate abdomen with mild tenderness to palpation in the right upper quadrant. There is no guarding or rebound tenderness. He has anormal bowel sounds and no palpable masses.

The patient's laboratory results show a normal complete blood count, electrolyte panel, and liver function tests. His serum amylase and lipase levels are within normal limits.

The patient has a history of chronic back pain for which he takes naproxen daily. He also takes lisinopril for hypertension and atorvastatin for hyperlipidemia.

The patient is anemic with a hemoglobin level of 11.3 g/dL. He has a low vitamin D level and takes supplements.

The patient has a history of sleep apnea and uses a continuous positive airway pressure machine.

The patient's electrocardiogram (ECG) shows a normal sinus rhythm. His echocardiogram reveals a normal left ventricular function and no valvular disease.

The patient has a history of anxiety and depression for which he takes sertraline.

The patient's surgical procedure was uneventful, and he tolerated the anesthesia well.

Postoperatively, the patient experienced some nausea and vomiting, which were managed with antiemetics.

The patient had a smooth postoperative course and was discharged home on the second postoperative day.

He was advised to continue his medications as before, except for naproxen, which was discontinued perioperatively.

The patient followed up with his surgeon one week after discharge and reported minimal right upper quadrant pain.

The patient's wound was healing well, without any signs of infection or dehiscence.

The patient was advised to avoid heavy lifting or bending for 2 weeks and to gradually resume his normal activities.

The patient was also advised to have a follow-up appointment with his primary care physician to monitor his blood pressure and lipid levels.

No complications were encountered during the perioperative period.
