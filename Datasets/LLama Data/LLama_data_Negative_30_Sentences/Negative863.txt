Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and left leg radiculopathy. The pain started after a work-related injury 2 years ago and has been persistent since then. The patient has tried physical therapy and medication but has not had significant relief.

The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia and is currently taking medication for both conditions. He has no history of cardiovascular disease or diabetes.

The patient is a current smoker and drinks occasionally. He has a history of substance abuse but has been sober for the past 5 years.

The patient underwent an MRI that showed a herniated disc at L4-L5 with compression of the left S1 nerve root.

The patient was scheduled for a lumbar discectomy.

Preoperatively, the patient received an epidural anesthesia and was started on intravenous antibiotics.

The surgery went uneventfully, and the patient was transferred to the postanesthesia care unit for recovery.

Postoperatively, the patient reported significant improvement in his pain and was able to ambulate without assistance.

The patient was discharged from the hospital 2 days after the surgery with a prescription for pain medication and instructions to follow up with his surgeon in 2 weeks.

Following his discharge, the patient experienced some nausea and vomiting but was otherwise feeling well.

The patient followed up with his surgeon 2 weeks after the surgery, and his incision was healing well.

The patient reported some numbness in his left foot but was otherwise happy with the outcome of the surgery.

The patient was advised to continue his pain medication and to follow up with his primary care physician for further management of his hypertension and hyperlipidemia.

The patient had a follow-up MRI 6 weeks after the surgery that showed significant improvement in the herniated disc and no evidence of nerve damage.

The patient was able to return to work 8 weeks after the surgery in a modified capacity.

The patient had a total of 3 postoperative appointments with his surgeon and primary care physician.

The patient had no complications during his postoperative course.
