Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and left leg radiculopathy. The pain has been persistent for the past 5 years and has been managed with physical therapy and medication. The patient has a history of smoking but quit 2 years ago. He also has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, which are well-controlled with medication.

The patient had a lumbar MRI that showed a large L4-L5 disc extrusion and mild spinal stenosis. He was referred to a neurosurgeon and was recommended for a L4-L5 decompression and fusion surgery.

The patient had a thorough preoperative evaluation, which included a physical examination, blood work, and an EKG. His bloodwork showed mild anemia and his EKG showed a prolonged QTc interval. He was referred to a cardiologist for clearance and was recommended to hold his blood thinner medication preoperatively.

The patient underwent a successful L4-L5 decompression and fusion surgery. His postoperative course was uneventful, and he did not experience any complications. He was discharged home on postoperative day 3 with instructions to avoid heavy lifting, bending, or twisting.

Following discharge, the patient had a postoperative appointment with his surgeon and reported minimal pain and numbness in his left leg. He was advised to continue with physical therapy to improve his mobility and strength. He also had a follow-up appointment with his primary care physician for blood work and EKG to monitor his anemia and QTc interval.

The patient had a smooth recovery and did not experience any complications such as infection, bleeding, or respiratory problems. He was satisfied with the outcome of the surgery and reported significant improvement in his pain and mobility.

Overall, the patient's surgical experience was successful, and he was able to return to his normal activities without any major complications.
