Patient Summary:

The patient is a 55-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic back pain and limited mobility. He has been diagnosed with spinal stenosis and has tried conservative management, including physical therapy and pain medication, without significant relief. The patient is a candidate for lumbar decompression surgery.

Medical History:

The patient has a history of hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and obesity. He had a myocardial infarction 10 years ago and has been compliant with his medications. He has no history of bleeding disorders or previous surgical interventions.

Surgical History:

The patient has had a cholecystectomy and a hernia repair in the past.

Current Medications:

The patient is taking atorvastatin, lisinopril, and oxycodone.

Allergies:

The patient is allergic to penicillin.

Social History:

The patient is a retired construction worker and has a 20-pack-year smoking history. He drinks alcohol occasionally.

Family History:

The patient's father died of a myocardial infarction at the age of 60. His mother has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia.

Review of Systems:

The patient reports numbness and tingling in his lower extremities, as well as difficulty ambulating and standing for long periods. He also experiences occasional shortness of breath.

Physical Exam:

The patient's blood pressure is 150/90 mmHg. His heart rate is 78 beats per minute. His respiratory rate is 16 breaths per minute. His temperature is 98.6°F (37°C).

Laboratory Results:

The patient's complete blood count and electrolyte levels are within normal limits. His creatinine kinase level is mildly elevated.

Imaging Studies:

The patient has undergone an MRI of the lumbar spine, which showed significant stenosis at L4-L5 and L5-S1.

Plan:

The patient will undergo a lumbar decompression surgery. The surgical team will consist of an anesthesiologist, a surgeon, and a nurse. The patient will receive general anesthesia and will be closely monitored during the procedure.

Postoperative Course:

The patient will be closely monitored in the postanesthesia care unit for the first 24 hours. He will receive pain management with narcotics and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs. He will be encouraged to ambulate as soon as possible and will be discharged to home once he is able to do so safely.

Complications:

The patient experienced some postoperative nausea and vomiting, which were managed with antiemetics. He also developed a superficial surgical site infection, which was treated with antibiotics. There were no other complications.

Follow-up:

The patient will follow up with his surgeon in one week and will have a postoperative MRI in two weeks to assess the efficacy of the surgery.
