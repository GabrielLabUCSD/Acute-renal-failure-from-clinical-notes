Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and bilateral leg pain. The pain has been gradually worsening over the past year, with radiation to the feet and numbness in the toes. The patient has tried various conservative treatments, including physical therapy and pain medication, but has not experienced significant relief.

The patient has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, and has been taking medication for both conditions. They have also been diagnosed with sleep apnea and use a continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) machine at night.

The patient is a current smoker and has a history of alcohol use disorder, but reports that they have been sober for the past year. They work as a construction worker and have been unable to perform their job due to the chronic pain.

On physical examination, the patient has limited mobility in the lower extremities and decreased sensation in the feet. They have a positive straight leg raise test and a positive femoral nerve stretch test.

Imaging studies include an MRI of the lumbar spine, which shows a large central herniated disc at L4-L5 and spinal stenosis. The patient has been referred to our service for consideration of a lumbar decompression and fusion surgery.

The patient is scheduled for a preoperative evaluation, which includes blood work, electrocardiogram, and a chest X-ray. They have been educated on the risks and benefits of the surgery, as well as the potential postoperative course.

Postoperative Course:

The patient underwent a successful lumbar decompression and fusion surgery. They were discharged from the hospital on postoperative day 3 and were advised to follow a strict rehabilitation protocol to ensure proper healing.

The patient experienced some expected postoperative pain and discomfort, which was managed with pain medication. They were also advised to avoid heavy lifting, bending, or twisting for the first 6 weeks after surgery.

During the postoperative follow-up, the patient reported some mild numbness and tingling in the feet, but this resolved over time. They were able to gradually resume their normal activities and return to work 8 weeks after surgery.

Complications:

The patient did not experience any major complications during the postoperative course. However, they did report some mild nausea and vomiting on postoperative day 1, which was managed with antiemetic medication.

The patient also developed a mild wound infection at the incision site, which was treated with antibiotics. They were advised to keep the wound clean and dry, and to avoid any strenuous activities that may exacerbate the infection.

In conclusion, the patient underwent a successful lumbar decompression and fusion surgery and experienced a smooth postoperative course. They were able to manage the expected postoperative pain and discomfort, and did not experience any major complications. The patient was able to return to work and resume their normal activities 8 weeks after surgery.
