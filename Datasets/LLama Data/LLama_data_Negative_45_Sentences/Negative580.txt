Patient Summary:

The patient is a 45-year-old male who presents with a history of chronic lower back pain and bilateral leg pain. The pain has been gradually worsening over the past 5 years and has been evaluated with conservative management, including physical therapy and pain medications, without significant relief.

The patient has a history of cigarette smoking, with a 20-pack-year history, and has been unable to quit despite numerous attempts. He also has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia, for which he takes medication regularly.

The patient underwent a lumbar MRI, which revealed a large central disc herniation at L4-L5 and mild spinal stenosis. He was Referred to a spine surgeon and decided to undergo a minimally invasive lumbar discectomy.

The patient had an uneventful preoperative course and was taken to the operating room without any complications. Intraoperatively, the patient had mild hypertension that was managed with titration of anesthesia drugs.

The surgery itself was uncomplicated, with successful removal of the disc herniation and decompression of the spinal cord. The patient was transferred to the postoperative recovery room in stable condition, with appropriate pain management and neurological function.

Postoperative Day 1:
The patient had a mild sore throat from the endotracheal tube but was otherwise comfortable. He had a minimal amount of nausea and was able to drink water without difficulty. The patient was encouraged to ambulate and did so without assistance.

Postoperative Day 2:
The patient had improved significantly, with minimal pain and increased mobility. He was able to walk around the hospital room without assistance and had a good appetite.

Postoperative Day 3:
The patient was transferred to a rehabilitation center for further recovery and physical therapy. He continued to improve, with gradual reduction in pain and increase in mobility.

Postoperative Week 1:
The patient had mild pain that was managed with oral medication and continued to improve with physical therapy.

Postoperative Week 2:
The patient had a mild episode of postoperative nausea that resolved with antiemetics. Otherwise, he continued to improve, with increased strength and mobility.

Postoperative Week 3:
The patient had a significant improvement in his pain and was able to ambulate without assistance. He was discharged home and advised to continue with outpatient physical therapy.

Postoperative Month 1:
The patient had a follow-up appointment with his surgeon and reported continued improvement in his symptoms. He was able to return to work part-time and had minimal pain.

Postoperative Month 2:
The patient had a further follow-up appointment and reported persistent improvement in his symptoms. He was able to return to work full-time and had minimal residual pain.

Overall, the patient had a successful postoperative course and was able to achieve significant improvement in his symptoms. He continued to smoke, but the surgery helped to alleviate the majority of his pain. The patient was counseled on the importance of smoking cessation to prevent future complications.

Complications:
None

The patient did not experience any complications during his postoperative course.
