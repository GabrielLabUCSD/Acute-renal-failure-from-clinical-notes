Acute renal failure (ARF) is a potentially life-threatening complication that can occur after surgery, especially in high-risk patients with pre-existing renal impairment or other factors that may impact renal function.

ARF is characterized by a sudden decline in renal function, leading to a buildup of waste products in the blood, such as creatinine and urea.

Risk factors for ARF include advanced age, preexisting renal disease, diabetes, hypertension, congestive heart failure, and peripheral vascular disease.

Other factors that may contribute to ARF include sepsis, shock, hypotension, and administration of nephrotoxic medications.

Intraoperative factors, such as hypotension, hypovolemia, and exposure to contrast agents, can also increase the risk of developing ARF.

Early recognition of ARF is critical to prevent further renal damage and optimize patient outcomes.

If ARF is suspected, prompt interventions should be implemented, such as fluid resuscitation, medication adjustments, and dialysis if necessary.
